Session 1 January 7

Becoming and Being Orthodox

We begin where every catechumen begins: with the question of what it actually means to be Orthodox. Not Orthodox by ethnicity or habit, but Orthodox by conviction, by baptism, by ongoing conversion. The word orthodoxia means right praise before it means right belief, and that ordering matters. In Orthodoxy, the way you worship shapes what you believe, not the other way around.

This session asks what it means to enter this Church, what you are committing to, and what the Church is committing to you. We look at the process of catechesis itself, why the ancient Church required extended formation before baptism, and what that tradition means for someone sitting in a parish hall in Jersey City in 2026.

Session 2 January 14

Christ's Way

The Sermon on the Mount · Prayer · Fasting · Almsgiving

The Sermon on the Mount is the most concentrated account of Christian ethics in the New Testament, and the Orthodox tradition has never read it as a list of rules. It is a description of what a human being looks like when God's life is in them. The Beatitudes are not requirements for entry. They are a portrait of the person who has already arrived.

We examine the three classical disciplines that structure Orthodox life: prayer, fasting, and almsgiving. These three have been inseparable since the Didache (c. 50 AD), and the reason is practical. The person who prays without fasting will find their prayer scattered. The person who fasts without giving will find their fasting self-regarding. Together, these practices are not achievements. They are a daily curriculum in dying to yourself so that someone else might live.

Session 3 January 21

Union with God and the Nature of the Church

The Orthodox theological tradition holds that the goal of human life is theosis: participation in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4). This is not absorption into God, not the erasure of the self, but a genuine communion between the uncreated God and created humanity. St. Athanasius put it plainly: God became man so that man might become god. This session unpacks what that means and why it matters that it sounds shocking.

The Church is not a building, a denomination, or a social club for the spiritually inclined. In Orthodox theology, the Church is the Body of Christ: the place where God and humanity meet, where theosis is made possible, where the future Kingdom is already present in the Eucharist. We examine how this understanding of the Church differs from what most Westerners inherited, and what it asks of the people who belong to it.

Session 4 January 28

Theology Proper

The Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit

The doctrine of the Trinity is not a theological puzzle invented by councils. It is the Church's attempt, worked out across four centuries of argument, prayer, and martyrdom, to say accurately what the New Testament actually claims about Jesus and the Spirit. The councils of Nicaea (325), Constantinople (381), Ephesus (431), and Chalcedon (451) did not invent Christian doctrine. They defended it against attempts to simplify it into something more philosophically comfortable.

We cover the Nicene Creed line by line: what each phrase means, why the Church fought over particular words, and why those words still matter. We also address the filioque controversy, which divided East and West in 1054 and remains unresolved. This is not just history. Understanding what the Church confesses about God is the foundation of everything else in the series.

Session 5 February 4

Sacraments

Conduits and Symbola of God's Presence

The Orthodox Church does not count sacraments and come up with seven because seven is a tidy number. The whole of the Church's life is sacramental: the physical world, transformed by the Incarnation, can now carry the life of God. Water can baptize. Oil can heal. Bread and wine can become the Body and Blood of Christ. This is not metaphor. The tradition insists it is real.

We look at Baptism and Chrismation as a single initiatory rite, at the Eucharist as the center around which everything else orbits, and at Holy Unction, Confession, Marriage, and Holy Orders as modes of God's presence in specific moments of human life. We also address what the Orthodox Church teaches about the Eucharist: what "real presence" means, how this understanding differs from both Roman Catholic transubstantiation and Protestant memorialism, and why it matters what you believe you are receiving when you approach the chalice.

Session 6 February 11

History of the Church 1

Pentecost through the Great Schism

The first thousand years of Christian history are not background. They are the substance of what the Orthodox Church is. We trace the Church from Pentecost through the apostolic mission, through persecution and martyrdom, through the legalization under Constantine and the complicated consequences of that moment, through the Ecumenical Councils, and into the Byzantine era.

This session pays particular attention to the Seven Ecumenical Councils (325 to 787), which define the Orthodox faith. We ask why the Church needed councils, what "ecumenical" means and doesn't mean, and how the conciliar tradition differs from the papal model that developed in Rome. We close with 1054: what the Great Schism was, what it was not, and why the wound has not healed.

Session 7 February 18

History of the Church 2

The Second Millennium through the OCA

The second millennium of Orthodox history is a story of survival. Constantinople falls in 1453, and Orthodox Christianity becomes the religion of subject peoples under Ottoman rule. It survives through the liturgy, through monasticism, through the courage of ordinary parishes. Russia emerges as the largest Orthodox nation, produces saints and theologians of the first order, and in 1917 suffers a catastrophe from which it is still recovering.

We trace Orthodoxy's arrival in America: the Russian mission to Alaska beginning in 1794, the great waves of immigrant parishes in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the founding of St. Vladimir's Seminary in 1938, the granting of autocephaly to the Orthodox Church in America in 1970. This is the lineage of the parish on Grand Street.

Session 8 February 25

Scripture in Tradition

The Source and Authority of our Faith

The Orthodox Church does not set Scripture against Tradition, as if Tradition were a second, competing authority. The Church reads Scripture as something the Tradition produced, preserves, and interprets. The New Testament canon was settled in the late 4th century by councils of bishops. Without the Church, there is no Bible. Without the Bible, the Church has nothing to say.

We cover how the Orthodox canon was formed, how the Church's Fathers interpreted Scripture (why the literal sense and the spiritual sense are both necessary), and what Orthodox hermeneutics looks like in practice. We also address the relationship between the Bible and the Liturgy: most Orthodox Christians hear the entirety of the New Testament read aloud in church over the course of a year. Scripture in the Orthodox tradition is primarily heard, not read privately, and that difference has consequences for how faith is received.

Session 9 March 4

Life, Death, Spirits, and the Spiritual World

Orthodox Christianity has a denser and more textured account of the invisible world than most modern Westerners expect. Angels are real, personal, and active. Demons are real. The saints are alive, present, and interceding. The departed are not gone. This session addresses all of it plainly, without embarrassment and without superstition.

We look at the Orthodox understanding of death: what happens at the moment of death, what the "toll-house" tradition means and where it comes from, why the Church prays for the departed, and what the resurrection of the body on the Last Day entails. We also discuss why the Orthodox do not hold a Western understanding of purgatory, what the distinction between particular and general judgment is, and why the Church's liturgical prayers express confidence in God's mercy without claiming certainty about the fate of any particular soul.

Session 10 March 11

All that is Holy

Worship · Icons · Saints · Liturgics

Orthodoxy is a liturgical religion. The Divine Liturgy is not a Sunday morning activity that accompanies the real life of faith. It is the real life of faith, condensed and made present. The full cycle of Orthodox worship, the daily cycle of offices, the weekly cycle of vespers and liturgy, and the annual cycle of fasting and feasting, is designed to immerse the believer in the death and resurrection of Christ until those become the natural rhythm of their life.

This session covers icons (what they are theologically, why the Church fought two iconoclast crises to keep them, and how to pray with them), the veneration of saints (why asking for the prayers of the departed is not worship of the saints), relics, the liturgical calendar, and the structure of the Orthodox year. Great Lent and Pascha receive particular attention: why the Orthodox keep Lent so seriously, and what the Paschal Vigil is meant to be.

Session 11 March 18

Church Practices

This session covers the practical habits of Orthodox life: how to make the sign of the cross, how to venerate an icon, how to receive a blessing from a priest, how to prepare for Holy Communion, how to make a confession, what the prostrations during Lent mean, and why these physical practices are not optional extras but integral to Orthodox prayer.

We also discuss fasting in more detail. The Orthodox fasting rule is extensive and can feel overwhelming to a newcomer. We talk about what the rule actually is, why it exists (the body is not an obstacle to prayer but its instrument), and how to approach the rule realistically without either ignoring it or turning it into a spiritual competition. How you eat on Wednesday is connected to how you pray on Sunday. That connection is the point.

Session 12 March 25

Family and Work

Orthodox Christianity has never been a religion for monasteries only. The lay vocation, lived in the middle of ordinary life, is a genuine path to God. This session addresses marriage, parenting, work, money, and civic life from an Orthodox perspective.

Marriage in the Orthodox tradition is not primarily a legal contract or even a romantic partnership. It is a small church: an icon of the love between Christ and the Church, ordered toward the salvation of husband and wife and the upbringing of children in the faith. We discuss what this means practically, how the Church's understanding of marriage differs from secular culture, and how the sacrament of Crowning captures both the joy and the weight of the commitment. We also address work: why the Orthodox tradition has always insisted that ordinary labor is not opposed to the spiritual life, how the creation mandate of Genesis still applies, and how to sanctify the hours of the working day.

Session 13 April 1

Church as Divine and Human

Perfect and Imperfect

We end where any honest reckoning with the Church must end: with the scandal of its failures. The Orthodox Church is the Body of Christ and it is populated entirely by people who are not yet saints. Clergy sin. Parishes fight. Jurisdictions fracture over money and ethnicity. Hierarchs make decisions that are cowardly or worse. None of this is news to God, and none of it changes what the Church is.

This final session addresses how to hold the Church's divine nature and its human failures at the same time, without either cynical detachment or naive idealism. We discuss what it means to be faithful to a community that disappoints you, why leaving is almost never the answer the tradition recommends, and what the saints who stayed in difficult circumstances actually looked like. We close with the question every catechumen has to answer for themselves: not whether the Church is perfect, but whether the risen Christ is present in it. If he is, then the door is open.
