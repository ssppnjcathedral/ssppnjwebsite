What Is the Divine Liturgy?

The Oldest Living
Encounter with God

The participation and unification of God's people in the Holy Mystery of Christ

Follow along in order or jump to any moment using the navigation above.

The Divine Liturgy is one of the most ancient and sacred Christian traditions, originating in the earliest days of the Church after the descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost. Rooted in the Apostolic era, this service embodies the central mystery of the Christian faith: the Eucharist, where bread and wine are transfigured to be the Body and Blood of Jesus Christ.

With its beginnings in upper rooms and catacombs, the Divine Liturgy was refined over several centuries, while preserving its deep spiritual meaning and communal significance - an unchanged essence of divine encounter and thanksgiving that unites the faithful with Christ and one another.

Through prayers, hymns, and sacred ritual, the Liturgy draws participants into the ongoing life of the Church, offering a foretaste of the heavenly banquet. The Liturgy reflects the patristic vision of worship as a dynamic and transformative encounter with God approached with faith, reverence, and active participation.

The Divine Liturgy is the high point of the daily cycle of services which include services like vespers, compline, nocturnes, matins, and the hours. This daily cycle of services is completed in a fuller manner at monasteries.

A Note on Worship

"The services of the church offer the experience, rare in modern America, of entering into something holy and healing, and which does not change to win us over, but changes us for union with the Highest."

Fifteen Sacred Moments

The Structure of the
Divine Liturgy

From the opening litany to the dismissal - one unbroken prayer
I
Part One

The Great Litany
and the Antiphons

Prayer for all people and hymns of ascent to the church

The service begins with the Great Litany, a series of petitions offered for the peace of the world, the well-being of the Church, and the salvation of all. The faithful respond to each petition with "Lord, have mercy," emphasizing the communal nature of prayer. This is followed by the Antiphons, psalm verses interspersed with refrains that highlight God's mercy and the joy of salvation. These prayers and hymns draw the congregation into a spirit of worship and set the tone for the rest of the Liturgy.

First Antiphon

The First Antiphon

Psalm verses sung as the faithful gather

Verses from the Psalms, set to music, are sung as the congregation gathers and the service begins. The antiphons gradually draw the faithful from the world outside into the spirit of worship, each one building on the last in devotional intensity.

Second Antiphon

The Second Antiphon

Concluding with the Only-Begotten Son

The second set of psalm verses continues the progression of prayer. It concludes with the Only-Begotten Son hymn, a brief but theologically profound summary of the Incarnation: "O Only-Begotten Son and Word of God, Who art immortal, yet didst deign for our salvation to be incarnate of the holy Theotokos and ever-virgin Mary."

Third Antiphon

The Third Antiphon

The Beatitudes, sung as the Little Entrance begins

The Beatitudes from Christ's Sermon on the Mount are chanted as the Little Entrance procession begins. They declare a life blessed not by worldly success, but by meekness, mercy, purity of heart, and union with God, setting the spiritual frame for everything that follows.

Historical Context

In the past, the antiphons were chanted by the congregation as they processed to the church. The Little Entrance brought the Gospel into the Church to begin the Liturgy itself.

II
Part Two

The Little Entrance

The beginning of the liturgy of the word

The Little Entrance is a solemn procession where the Gospel Book is carried to the altar, symbolizing Christ's entry into the world and His presence among His people. The clergy lead this act with great reverence, as the people sing the beatitudes, the cornerstone of Christ's Sermon on the Mount, which upends worldly notions of a good life and declares a life blessed by likeness to, and union with, the life of God. This moment marks the transition to the proclamation of God's Word.

Scripture and the Community

The Old Testament and New Testament scriptures were originally read almost exclusively as a communal act, and the services of the church remained the primary opportunity to learn scripture for many centuries.

III
Part Three

The Proclamation
of Scripture

The Word put in words

Central to the Liturgy is the reading of Scripture. The Epistle is read first, followed by the chanting of the Alleluia, and then the Gospel, proclaimed with great reverence by the deacon or priest. The Word of God nourishes the faithful, offering guidance, inspiration, and truth. After the readings, the Homily is delivered, providing interpretation and practical application of the scriptures for the spiritual growth of the congregation. The homily may also include reflections on a saint or event remembered on a given day, or on the liturgical season.

Troparia

Troparia

Hymns of the feast or saint of the day

Troparia are short, poetic hymns proper to the feast or saint being commemorated. Sung between the Little Entrance and the Scripture readings, they orient the congregation to the particular celebration of the day, offering a brief theology of the feast in musical form.

The Epistle

The Epistle

A reading from the Apostolic letters

A passage is read from the Acts of the Apostles or the letters of Paul, Peter, James, or John. The reader chants the appointed passage in a tone specific to the liturgical tradition. Before the reading, a Prokeimenon, a verse from the Psalms, is chanted antiphonally as a preparation.

The Holy Gospel

The Holy Gospel

The high point of the Liturgy of the Word

The Gospel reading is the culmination of the Liturgy of the Word. The faithful stand in reverence, the same posture as at the Resurrection, as the deacon or priest proclaims the appointed passage. The Alleluia is chanted before it in preparation. The Homily follows, applying the Word of God to the life of the congregation.

Historical Note

A Tradition of Attentiveness

The Divine Liturgy used to be 4 hours long

Compared to the longer Divine Liturgy of St. Basil the Great, and especially the full setting of the Divine Liturgy of St. James of Jerusalem, which are celebrated less frequently, St. John's version reduces the length of prayers while retaining great richness. The Liturgy may challenge unformed attention spans; this is one of its blessings.

Historical Context

The Old Testament and New Testament scriptures were originally read almost exclusively as a communal act, and the services of the church remained the primary opportunity to learn scripture for many centuries.

IV
Part Four

Litany of Fervent
Supplication

Intensified prayer on behalf of all

Following the Homily, the Litany of Fervent Supplication intensifies the communal prayer of the Church. Where the Great Litany responds with "Lord, have mercy" once, here the faithful respond three times, a triple invocation expressing deeper urgency and contrition. The deacon or priest offers petitions for the Church, her clergy, and the faithful, and the congregation responds with heightened fervor. This litany marks the transition from the Liturgy of the Word toward the Eucharistic prayers of the faithful.

Kyrie Eleison

The triple response "Lord, have mercy" in Greek is Kyrie, eleison, eleison, eleison. The tripling of the response is an ancient intensification of the petition, an expression that the congregation is calling on God not as a formality, but with the full weight of their need.

V
Part Five

Litany for
the Catechumens

Prayer for those preparing to enter the Church

In the early Church, those preparing for Holy Baptism, called catechumens, were present for the first part of the Liturgy but were dismissed before the Eucharistic prayers began. The Liturgy retains this ancient practice. A special litany is offered for the catechumens, praying for their instruction, preparation, and reception into the faith. At its conclusion, they are formally dismissed with the words "Catechumens, depart." Today this moment also calls the faithful to reflect on their own ongoing conversion, reminding all that the life of the Church is one of continual growth toward God.

An Ancient Practice

In earlier centuries the dismissal of catechumens was a solemn and literal act, the doors of the church were closed and guarded. The Liturgy that followed was considered so sacred that it was not to be witnessed by those not yet baptized into the Body of Christ.

VI
Part Six

Litanies of
the Faithful

The Church prepares to offer the Holy Gifts

With the catechumens departed, two short litanies draw the congregation into final preparation for the Eucharistic offering. These Litanies of the Faithful ask God to cleanse the souls and bodies of the faithful and to make them worthy to offer the Holy Gifts. In ancient practice, the doors of the church were closed at this moment, only the baptized remained. They mark the solemn threshold between the Liturgy of the Word and the Liturgy of the Faithful, the heart of the Divine Liturgy.

Two Natures of the Liturgy

The transition from the Litanies of the Faithful to the Great Entrance marks the boundary between the two great halves of the Liturgy, the Liturgy of the Word, open to all, and the Liturgy of the Faithful, the Eucharistic mystery reserved for the baptized.

VII
Part Seven

The Great Entrance

Commemorating the procession of Christ's body to the tomb

The Great Entrance is a procession in which the bread and wine, prepared during the Prothesis, are carried to the altar. This act signifies the offering of the gifts by the Church and mirrors the journey of Christ to His voluntary sacrifice. As the clergy chant prayers for the faithful, the gifts are placed on the altar, where they will be consecrated. The congregation joins in by singing hymns of devotion, preparing their hearts for the mystery to come.

The Catechumens

In the earlier centuries, catechumens - those preparing to join the church by baptism - would leave shortly after the homily for a time of instruction.

VIII
Part Eight

Litany of Supplication

Asking God to receive the offered gifts

Having placed the Holy Gifts on the altar, the priest leads the congregation in the Litany of Supplication, a prayer asking God to receive the offering, to protect the faithful through the day, and to grant them an angel of peace and every good gift. The faithful respond "Grant this, O Lord" to each petition, expressing their trust in God's mercy and their willingness to place all things in His hands as the Eucharistic mystery draws near.

An Angel of Peace

Among the petitions of the Litany of Supplication is the request for "an angel of peace, a faithful guide, a guardian of our souls and bodies." This ancient prayer reflects the patristic understanding that the Liturgy opens the faithful to the presence of the heavenly hosts.

IX
Part Nine

The Passing
of the Peace

Greeting one another in Christ before the Creed

Before the Creed is proclaimed, the clergy exchange the kiss of peace, an ancient gesture rooted in St. Paul's command to "greet one another with a holy kiss." In early Christian practice, the entire congregation exchanged this greeting as a sign that they were united in faith and charity before offering the Eucharist together. Today the clergy exchange the peace in the altar, while the faithful greet one another, recalling that reconciliation with one another is inseparable from union with God, and that no one may approach the chalice while holding grievance against a brother or sister.

Scripture

"If you are offering your gift at the altar and there remember that your brother has something against you, leave your gift there before the altar and go. First be reconciled to your brother, and then come and offer your gift." (Matthew 5:23-24)

X
Part Ten

The Creed

The faith of the Church spoken with one voice

The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed, proclaimed by the entire congregation together, is the doctrinal heart of the Divine Liturgy. Formulated at the Councils of Nicaea (325 AD) and Constantinople (381 AD), it expresses the Orthodox faith in the Holy Trinity, Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. Every baptized believer affirms this faith before the Eucharistic prayer begins, making the Creed both a personal profession and a communal act of witness. The veil of the altar is drawn back as the Creed is sung, signifying the openness of heaven to the faithful, a foretaste of what the Anaphora will make present.

The Council of Nicaea, 325 AD

The Nicene Creed emerged from the first Ecumenical Council, called to address the Arian controversy. Its precise language, affirming that the Son is "of one essence with the Father", became the touchstone of Orthodox Christology and remains the universal confession of Christian faith to this day.

XI
Part Eleven

The Anaphora

The "lifting up" of God's gifts for sanctification

The Anaphora is the heart of the Divine Liturgy. In this Eucharistic prayer, the priest recalls Christ's saving work and invokes the Holy Spirit to sanctify the gifts of bread and wine, transfiguring them into the Body and Blood of Christ. This moment is the pinnacle of the service, where heaven and earth meet, and the faithful are invited to participate in the sacred mystery of the Eucharist. The Lord's Prayer follows, identifying the congregation, in Christ's own words, with "our" Father by participation in Christ, the Son.

St. Justin Martyr, 150 AD

"And this food is called among us Eucharist, of which no one is allowed to partake but the man who believes that the things which we teach are true... For we have been taught that the prayer is the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh."

XII
Part Twelve

Litany Before
the Lord's Prayer

A final petition before approaching the chalice

As the faithful prepare to receive Holy Communion, a brief Litany of Supplication is offered, asking God's mercy and grace upon all who are about to approach the chalice. This moment of communal prayer creates a final threshold, a pause before the most intimate act of the Liturgy. The faithful are called to examine their hearts, to approach with reverence, and to trust entirely in God's mercy rather than in their own worthiness.

Preparation for Communion

Orthodox Christians prepare for Holy Communion through fasting, prayer, and confession. The Litany Before the Lord's Prayer is the communal expression of that preparation, the congregation placing itself before God in humility before receiving His most holy gift.

XIII
Part Thirteen

The Lord's Prayer

Praying as one Body before receiving Communion

The entire congregation prays the Lord's Prayer together, the prayer Christ Himself taught His disciples, immediately before Holy Communion. In the context of the Liturgy, the words take on their fullest meaning: "our Father" is spoken not as individuals, but as members of one Body. "Give us this day our daily bread" is understood by the Fathers as a direct reference to the Eucharist itself. "Forgive us our trespasses" calls each communicant to approach the chalice in humility and contrition, having already been reconciled with their brothers and sisters at the Passing of the Peace.

Our Daily Bread

St. Cyril of Jerusalem wrote: "When He says 'Give us this day our daily bread,' we understand this bread as spiritual bread. For ordinary bread is not 'super-substantial,' but this holy Bread is super-substantial, that is, appointed for the substance of the soul." The Lord's Prayer, prayed here, is a prayer for the Eucharist itself.

XIV
Part Fourteen

Holy Communion

Sharing in Christ

After the gifts are consecrated, the faithful receive Holy Communion, the Body and Blood of Christ, which are ever united to His divinity. This sacred act is a profound expression of unity with God and the Church. The faithful approach the chalice with reverence and preparation, having examined their hearts and confessed their sins. Communion is both a personal and communal act, drawing each participant closer to Christ and one another: we live in Him and we are the branches of Christ the vine (John 6:48-59 & 15:5).

The Word of God

"I am the bread of life. Whoever feeds on my flesh and drinks my blood has eternal life... I am the vine; you are the branches." (John 6:48-54; 15:5)

XV
Part Fifteen

Thanksgiving
and Dismissal

Departing in peace to expand these sacred hours to the rest of life

Following Communion, the priest leads the congregation in prayers of thanksgiving, acknowledging God's grace and the gift of the Eucharist. The service concludes with the Dismissal, where the priest blesses the faithful, sending them forth to live as witnesses of Christ in the world. A piece of blessed bread, called antidoron, is distributed. This is eaten carefully (without dropping crumbs), but is not communion, and is not restricted to only the faithful.

The Work of the People

The word "liturgy" comes from the Greek leitourgia, meaning "the work of the people." The dismissal sends the faithful to continue that work in every corner of their lives.

The Church Calendar

When Do Services Occur?

The structure of the Church calendar

The Divine Liturgy, the service this guide describes, is part of the daily cycle of services. It is the service at which communion is celebrated, and is the service participated in by all the faithful on Sunday mornings. Other services, which match the ancient reckoning of the hours of the day and significant hours in the passion of Christ, are generally broken up in the following way: Ninth Hour, Vespers, and Compline; Nocturnes (Midnight Service), Matins, and First Hour; Third and Sixth Hours followed by Divine Liturgy. If arriving before Divine Liturgy on Sunday, you will hear the psalms and prayers of the 3rd and 6th hours.

The fullest experience of these services is found in monasteries, where multiple services occur daily, but most parishes will offer evening Vespers and Divine Liturgy on a typical week which does not include special feasts. Every day commemorates a number of saints and events, but especially honored are the 12 great feasts from the life of Christ and his mother. The Resurrection of Christ; Pascha (which is the Greek for Passover and known as Easter in English speaking countries) is the greatest feast and sets much of the pace of the calendar each year.

The Liturgical Tradition

The Liturgy of St. Basil

The foundation of the Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom

The Liturgy of St. Basil is occasionally served during the year, particularly on Sundays during Great Lent. The earlier Liturgy of St. Basil is very similar to St. John Chrysostom's except for the replacement of certain hymns and prayers, and especially much longer silent prayers by the priest. There are also Vesperal Liturgies, which combine the evening service of Vespers with the Liturgy of St. Basil. Also, the "Presanctified" Liturgy is served on Wednesdays and Fridays in Lent; this liturgy (typically served in the evening) has a unique character, with special solemnity and many prostrations. Other similarly ancient liturgies also exist in the Orthodox world, descending from the apostolic and patristic tradition in various regions. The Liturgy according to St. John Chrysostom is by far the most celebrated Liturgy of the Eastern Orthodox Church, and is therefore outlined here.

Antidoron

The blessed bread distributed at the end of the Liturgy is a sign of fellowship and welcome. Unlike communion, it is offered to all who attend, regardless of faith. It is eaten with care and gratitude.

The Orthodox calendar honors twelve feasts especially: Nativity of the Theotokos, Exaltation of the Cross, Presentation of the Theotokos, Nativity of Christ, Theophany, Presentation of Christ, Annunciation, Palm Sunday, Ascension, Pentecost, Transfiguration, and Dormition of the Theotokos.

Saint Basil the Great · Archbishop of Caesarea, 329–379 AD Saint Basil the Great

Born around AD 329 and reposing in the Lord in AD 379, St. Basil was an influential early Church Father and bishop of Caesarea in Cappadocia. Celebrated for defending Orthodox Christianity against the Arian heresy, founding monastic communities, and authoring key theological works. His Liturgy was compiled and reformed during his episcopate in the fourth century, evolving from earlier apostolic traditions with expansions influenced by Christological debates and synodal decisions.

The Presanctified Liturgy

Served on Wednesday and Friday evenings in Great Lent, the Liturgy of the Presanctified Gifts uses Gifts consecrated at a previous Liturgy. Its atmosphere is one of deep penitence and longing - a liturgy shaped entirely by the season of preparation.

"Let us who mystically represent the Cherubim, and who sing the thrice-holy hymn to the life-creating Trinity, now lay aside all earthly cares."

The Cherubic Hymn · Chanted at the Great Entrance · Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom
