Ancient Roots

The History of the Church: An Introduction

The original Christian Faith — twenty centuries of unbroken witness

The Orthodox Church was founded by our Lord Jesus Christ and is the living manifestation of His presence in the history of mankind. The most conspicuous characteristics of Orthodoxy are its rich liturgical life and its faithfulness to the apostolic tradition. It is believed by Orthodox Christians that our Church has preserved the tradition and continuity of the ancient Church in its fullness compared to other Christian denominations which have departed, to smaller or greater degrees, from the common tradition of the Church of the first ten centuries.

Historical Points

◆ 300 Million Christians worldwide

Orthodox Population

Today the Orthodox Church numbers approximately 300 million Christians who follow the faith and practices defined by the first seven ecumenical councils. The word orthodox — "right belief and right glory" — has traditionally designated communities who preserved the true faith as opposed to those declared heretical. The official designation of the Church in its liturgical and canonical texts is "the Orthodox Catholic Church."

✝ 14 Autocephalous churches

Autocephalous Churches

The Orthodox Church is a family of self-governing churches, with the Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople holding honorary primacy as primus inter pares — first among equals. The Church is not a centralized organization headed by a pontiff. Its unity is manifested in common faith and communion in the sacraments. Christ himself is the head of the Church. Today the autocephalous churches include Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, Jerusalem, Russia, Serbia, Romania, Bulgaria, Georgia, Cyprus, Greece, Poland, Albania, and America.

★ Primus Inter Pares First among equals

The Patriarchates

The order of precedence among the autocephalous churches does not reflect actual influence or numerical importance. There remains a consensus that Constantinople's primacy of honor — recognized by the ancient canons because it was the capital of the Byzantine Empire — should remain as a symbol and tool of church unity. Orthodoxy is not merely an earthly organization: it is the mystical Body of Christ, whose composition includes not only clergy but all who truly believe and have entered through Holy Baptism.

⊕ AD 1054 The Great Schism

The Great Schism

The Schism between the Eastern and Western Church was the result of centuries of estrangement — linguistic, cultural, and political. At its root were two incompatible concepts of church authority. The Roman claim to immediate jurisdictional primacy was incompatible with the traditional Orthodox understanding, which held all churches as sister churches and recognized the Roman bishop only as first among equals. For the East, the highest authority in doctrinal disputes was never a single bishop, but an Ecumenical Council of all the churches.

♦ One Faith Divided over centuries

Differences in Doctrine

Over time the Church of Rome adopted teachings not thoroughly supported in the Tradition, culminating in the proclamation of papal infallibility. Protestant communities which later split from Rome diverged further still. Many Orthodox theologians hold that the differences are not merely cultural but are a direct consequence of a gradual departure from sacred tradition. This is why Orthodox Christians want their family and friends to join them — not from exclusivity, but from the genuine conviction that they have found a treasure worth sharing.

☉ Every Continent Orthodox presence worldwide

Orthodox Diaspora

After the Schism of 1054, Orthodoxy's heartland remained the Middle East, the Balkans, and Russia — with Constantinople as its center. Missionary expansion into Asia and emigration to the West carried Orthodoxy to every corner of the globe. Saints Peter and Paul in Jersey City is itself a product of that diaspora — a parish founded by immigrant faithful who brought the ancient faith with them and kept it alive for generations.

✠ 17 Centuries Of unbroken monasticism

Monasticism

The Orthodox Church is well known for its developed monasticism, traceable from the Egyptian desert monasteries of the 3rd and 4th centuries — themselves inspired by the prophets and St. John the Baptist. Monasticism spread through Palestine, Syria, Cappadocia, Gaul, Ireland, Italy, Greece, and the Slavic lands. Allowing single-minded attention to the Kingdom of God, it has always been a beacon of Orthodoxy and continues to make a lasting impact on Orthodox spirituality worldwide.

☩ 1,600 Years The same Divine Liturgy

The Divine Liturgical Tradition

The Orthodox Church is an invaluable treasury of liturgical tradition handed down from the earliest centuries of Christianity. The beauty and grandeur of the Divine Liturgy make the presence of heaven on earth real and immediate. Orthodox art and music serve a functional role in the liturgy — helping even the bodily senses to feel the spiritual grandeur of the mysteries. Icons are not merely art: they are a means through which we experience the reality of the Heavenly Kingdom, conduits through which heavenly assistance comes.

Recommended Reading

Books that have guided countless people into the Orthodox Faith
Begin Here

Essential first books for anyone curious about Orthodoxy

The Orthodox Way Kallistos Ware

The single most recommended starting point. Covers theology, prayer, and the spiritual life with warmth and accessibility. Begin here.

The Orthodox Church Kallistos Ware

The companion to The Orthodox Way — more historical and institutional. Covers the history, structure, and faith of the Church from the early centuries to today.

Surprised by Christ Fr. James Bernstein

A convert's memoir — one of the most compelling accounts of a journey into Orthodoxy. Especially resonant for those coming from Protestant backgrounds.

Becoming Orthodox Fr. Peter Gillquist

The story of a large Protestant community's decade-long journey into the Orthodox Church. Practical, honest, and deeply moving.

The Religion of the Apostles Fr. Stephen De Young

Traces Orthodox Christianity back to its apostolic roots in first-century Judaism. An eye-opening case that the Faith was complete from the beginning.

Saint Paul the Pharisee Fr. Stephen De Young

A deep reading of St. Paul's letters through the lens of Second Temple Judaism and Orthodox tradition. Reframes Paul as a thoroughly Jewish apostle whose theology is inseparable from the Old Testament world.

Orthodoxy and Heterodoxy Fr. Andrew Stephen Damick

A clear-eyed guide to how Orthodoxy relates to other Christian traditions — Catholic, Protestant, and beyond. Charitable, thorough, and essential for anyone coming from another background.

Go Deeper

For those ready to engage Orthodox theology seriously

For the Life of the World Fr. Alexander Schmemann

A slim, essential book about the sacramental vision of the world. Changes how you see everything. One of the most important Orthodox texts of the 20th century.

The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church Vladimir Lossky

The foundational theological introduction to the Eastern tradition. Essential for anyone who wants to understand Orthodox thought at its depth.

Deification in Christ Panayiotis Nellas

A profound study of Orthodox anthropology and theosis — what it means for the human person to be made in the image of God and to grow into His likeness.

St. Maximus the Confessor Andrew Louth

The best single-volume introduction to the greatest Byzantine theologian. Louth translates key texts and provides a lucid guide to Maximus's cosmic vision.

The Ethics of Beauty Timothy Patitsas

A remarkable work of Orthodox moral theology arguing that beauty is the proper ground of Christian ethics. Dense, rewarding, and original.

Christ the Conqueror of Hell Metropolitan Hilarion Alfeyev

A deep study of Christ's descent into Hades. Presents a message of cosmic hope using Scripture, patristic tradition, and liturgical texts.

Orthodox Christianity (5 vols.) Metropolitan Hilarion Alfeyev

The most comprehensive modern survey of Orthodox theology, history, worship, and spiritual life. A monumental reference for serious students of the faith.

Spiritual Classics

The enduring texts of Orthodox prayer and the inner life

The Way of a Pilgrim Anonymous

A 19th-century Russian pilgrim's account of learning the Jesus Prayer. Short, luminous, and transformative. One of the most beloved books in Orthodox literature.

The Mountain of Silence Kyriacos C. Markides

A sociologist follows a Mount Athos monk to Cyprus and discovers the depth of Orthodox mystical spirituality. Accessible, deeply moving, and an ideal introduction for Western seekers.

The Philokalia Various Desert Fathers

The collected writings of the Desert Fathers on prayer and the inner life. Not read cover to cover — dipped into. Begin with Volume 1. A lifetime's companion.

Therapy of Spiritual Illness Jean-Claude Larchet

A vast synthesis of patristic teaching on the passions, healing, and salvation. Presents Orthodox asceticism as a complete science of the soul.

Elder Paisios of Mount Athos Hieromonk Isaac

The spiritual counsels of one of the most beloved saints of the 20th century. Practical, warm, occasionally astonishing. Beloved across the Orthodox world.

On the Incarnation St. Athanasius the Great

The foundational text on why God became man. Written in the 4th century, it reads with startling clarity. C.S. Lewis called it a masterpiece and wrote the introduction to the popular English edition — a fitting entry point.

On the Orthodox Faith St. John of Damascus

The definitive systematic theology of the Eastern Church, written in the 8th century. Still the standard reference for Orthodox doctrine. Dense but essential for anyone who wants to understand the faith at its foundations.
