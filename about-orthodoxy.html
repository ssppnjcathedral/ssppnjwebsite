Introduction

What Is Orthodox Christianity?

The original Christian Church, preserved in fullness through the ages

If you have found your way to this page, you are probably asking a question. Maybe it began with a service you attended — the incense, the chanting, the icons — and you left with more questions than answers. Maybe you read something, or a friend invited you, or you have been circling Orthodoxy for years and aren't sure how to take the next step.

Whatever brought you here, you are welcome. Orthodox Christianity has been welcoming seekers since the day of Pentecost. This page is an introduction — a map, not the territory.

A Brief History

Orthodox Christianity traces its origin to Pentecost — the day described in the Acts of the Apostles when the Holy Spirit descended upon the disciples and the Church was born. From Jerusalem, the Faith spread through the Roman Empire and beyond: to Alexandria, Antioch, Constantinople, and Rome. For the first thousand years, the Church was undivided.

In 1054, the Great Schism separated the Latin West (which became Roman Catholicism) from the Greek and Slavic East (which continued as Eastern Orthodoxy). The Orthodox Church did not reform itself, did not revise its doctrines, and did not change its liturgy. It continued as it had been — the living continuation of the Apostolic Church.

Today, the Orthodox Church has approximately 300 million members worldwide — the second-largest Christian body on earth. It includes Greek, Russian, Serbian, Romanian, Antiochian, and other national churches, all sharing the same Faith, the same liturgy, and the same bishops in unbroken apostolic succession from the Apostles.

Common Questions

Honest answers to the questions inquirers most often ask
Can I attend a service without being Orthodox?
Absolutely. All are welcome at our services. You do not need to be Orthodox, baptized, or even a believer to walk through our doors. The only thing you cannot do as a non-Orthodox is receive Holy Communion — but you can be present, listen, pray, observe, and ask questions afterward. Many of our parishioners attended services for months or years before making any formal commitment.
Is Orthodox Christianity the same as Catholic?
Orthodox and Roman Catholic Christianity share a common origin and many similarities — both have the Nicene Creed, a sacramental life, a priesthood, and a commitment to the Councils of the early Church. The differences center primarily on the Papacy (Orthodox Christianity has no equivalent to the Pope), the filioque controversy (a theological dispute about the Holy Spirit), and certain liturgical and canonical differences. Eastern Catholics (sometimes called "Greek Catholics") are in communion with Rome; Orthodox are not.
What happens during the Divine Liturgy?
The Divine Liturgy is the central act of Orthodox Christian worship. It follows a structure that has been essentially unchanged for over a thousand years: the Liturgy of the Word (scripture readings, homily), and the Liturgy of the Faithful (the Eucharist). Services are sung, not spoken. Icons surround the worshippers. Incense is offered. The priest moves in and out of the altar through the Royal Doors. The whole body is involved — standing, bowing, making the sign of the Cross. It typically lasts between 90 minutes and two hours.
How does someone become Orthodox?
There is no fixed timeline. Most people go through a period of inquiry — attending services, reading, asking questions, meeting with the priest. When a person is ready to formally enter the Church, they receive the Mysteries of Initiation: Baptism (if not previously baptized), Chrismation (the anointing with holy oil, analogous to Confirmation), and first Holy Communion. The process is personal and unhurried. Father Solomon is glad to meet with anyone who is interested.
Do I need to speak a foreign language?
No. At Saints Peter & Paul, our services are conducted primarily in English, with some Slavonic elements preserved for their liturgical beauty. You will be able to follow the service and understand what is happening.
What about fasting and other practices?
Orthodox Christianity has a rich tradition of fasting — abstaining from meat, fish, dairy, wine, and oil on certain days and during certain seasons (especially Great Lent before Pascha). Fasting is a discipline, not a requirement for attendance. Inquirers are encouraged to explore these practices gradually, with guidance from a priest, rather than attempting everything at once.

Take the Next Step

The best way to learn about Orthodoxy is to come to a service. We gather every Saturday at 5:00 PM for Vespers and every Sunday at 9:30 AM for the Divine Liturgy. You are welcome at either — no preparation or prior knowledge required.
