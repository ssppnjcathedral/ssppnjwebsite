The Beginning of Vespers

Opening Prayers

Blessing, invocation, and the Lord's Prayer

Great Vespers is served every Saturday evening and on the eve of any feast when the Menaion or Pentecostarion appoints Old or New Testament lessons. It begins with the blessing of God and a series of preparatory prayers that gather the faithful out of the noise of the day and into the presence of the holy.

If the Ninth Hour was not said immediately before, the priest opens with the blessing. Otherwise the reader begins at "Come, let us worship" on the next section.

Priest: Blessed is our God always, now and ever and unto ages of ages.

Reader: Amen. Glory to Thee, our God, glory to Thee.

O Heavenly King, the Comforter, the Spirit of truth, Who art everywhere and fillest all things; Treasury of blessings and Giver of life: come and abide in us and cleanse us from every impurity, and save our souls, O Good One.

Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal, have mercy on us. (thrice)

Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages. Amen.

O Most Holy Trinity, have mercy on us. O Lord, cleanse us from our sins. O Master, pardon our transgressions. O Holy One, visit and heal our infirmities, for Thy Name's sake.

Lord, have mercy. (thrice)

Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages. Amen.

Our Father, Who art in heaven, hallowed be Thy Name; Thy Kingdom come; Thy will be done on earth, as it is in heaven. Give us this day our daily bread, and forgive us our trespasses, as we forgive those who trespass against us; and lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from evil.

Priest: For Thine is the Kingdom, and the power, and the glory of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.

Reader: Amen. Lord, have mercy. (12x)

Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages. Amen.

On Evening Prayer

Vespers is the first service of the liturgical day. In the ancient reckoning, the new day begins at sunset, so Saturday evening Vespers opens the celebration of Sunday, the Day of Resurrection.

I
Part One

Psalm 103

A hymn of creation sung at the door of the evening

The service begins properly with the great creation psalm, Psalm 103, chanted while the priest silently reads the Prayers of the Lamp before the iconostasis. The psalm sings of the world as God made it: light, water, mountains, creatures, and the turning of day into night. It places the entire evening prayer in the frame of thanksgiving for creation.

Come, let us worship God our King. Come, let us worship and fall down before Christ, our King and our God. Come, let us worship and fall down before Christ Himself, our King and our God.

Psalm 103

Bless the Lord, O my soul. O Lord my God, Thou art very great. Thou art clothed with honor and majesty, Who coverest Thyself with light as with a garment, Who hast stretched out the heavens like a tent, Who hast laid the beams of Thy chambers on the waters, Who makest the clouds Thy chariot, Who ridest on the wings of the wind, Who makest the winds Thy messengers, fire and flame Thy ministers.

Thou didst set the earth on its foundations, so that it should never be shaken. Thou didst cover it with the deep as with a garment; the waters stood above the mountains. At Thy rebuke they fled; at the sound of Thy thunder they took to flight. The mountains rose, the valleys sank down to the place which Thou didst appoint for them. Thou didst set a bound which they should not pass, so that they might not again cover the earth.

Thou makest springs gush forth in the valleys; they flow between the hills, they give drink to every beast of the field; the wild asses quench their thirst. By them the birds of the air have their habitation; they sing among the branches. From Thy lofty abode Thou waterest the mountains; the earth is satisfied with the fruit of Thy work.

Thou dost cause the grass to grow for the cattle, and plants for man to cultivate, that he may bring forth food from the earth, and wine to gladden the heart of man, oil to make his face shine, and bread to strengthen man's heart. The trees of the Lord are watered abundantly, the cedars of Lebanon which He planted. In them the birds build their nests; the stork has her home in the fir trees. The high mountains are for the wild goats; the rocks are a refuge for the badgers.

Thou hast made the moon to mark the seasons; the sun knows its time for setting. Thou makest darkness, and it is night, when all the beasts of the forest creep forth. The young lions roar for their prey, seeking their food from God. When the sun rises, they get them away and lie down in their dens. Man goes forth to his work and to his labor until the evening.

O Lord, how manifold are Thy works. In wisdom hast Thou made them all; the earth is full of Thy creatures. Yonder is the sea, great and wide, which teems with things innumerable, living things both small and great. There go the ships, and Leviathan which Thou didst form to sport in it. These all look to Thee, to give them their food in due season. When Thou givest to them, they gather it up; when Thou openest Thy hand, they are filled with good things. When Thou hidest Thy face, they are dismayed; when Thou takest away their breath, they die and return to their dust. When Thou sendest forth Thy Spirit, they are created; and Thou renewest the face of the ground.

May the glory of the Lord endure forever, may the Lord rejoice in His works, Who looks on the earth and it trembles, Who touches the mountains and they smoke. I will sing to the Lord as long as I live; I will sing praise to my God while I have being. May my meditation be pleasing to Him, for I rejoice in the Lord. Let sinners be consumed from the earth, and let the wicked be no more. Bless the Lord, O my soul.

And again:

The sun knows its time for setting. Thou makest darkness, and it is night. O Lord, how manifold are Thy works. In wisdom hast Thou made them all.

Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages. Amen.

Alleluia, alleluia, alleluia, glory to Thee, O God. (thrice)

The Creation Psalm

Psalm 103 is sung at every Vespers in the Byzantine tradition. It places the entire evening prayer within creation itself, and within time as God governs it. The setting sun is not loss but the gift of rest and the promise of a new day.

II
Part Two

The Great Litany

Intercession for the peace of the whole world

When the reader has finished the psalm, the deacon or priest begins the Great Litany, a series of petitions offered for the peace of the world, the Church, civil authorities, travelers, the sick, and all people. The faithful respond to each petition with "Lord, have mercy," the ancient prayer of the Church drawn from the Psalms.

Deacon (Priest): In peace, let us pray to the Lord.

R. Lord, have mercy. (after each petition)

For the peace from above and for the salvation of our souls, let us pray to the Lord.

For the peace of the whole world, for the welfare of the holy Churches of God, and for the union of all, let us pray to the Lord.

For this holy house and for those who enter it with faith, reverence, and the fear of God, let us pray to the Lord.

For our Metropolitan N., for our Bishop N., for the honorable priesthood, the diaconate in Christ, and for all the clergy and the people, let us pray to the Lord.

For this country, its President, for all civil authorities, and for the armed forces, let us pray to the Lord.

For this city, for every city and countryside, and for the faithful dwelling in them, let us pray to the Lord.

For seasonable weather, for abundance of the fruits of the earth, and for peaceful times, let us pray to the Lord.

For travelers by land, by sea, and by air; for the sick and the suffering; for captives and their salvation, let us pray to the Lord.

That we may be delivered from all affliction, wrath, danger, and necessity, let us pray to the Lord.

Help us, save us, have mercy on us, and keep us, O God, by Thy grace.

Commemorating our most holy, most pure, most blessed and glorious Lady Theotokos and Ever-virgin Mary with all the saints, let us commend ourselves and each other, and all our life unto Christ our God.

R. To Thee, O Lord.

Priest: For unto Thee are due all glory, honor, and worship: to the Father and to the Son and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.

R. Amen.

Kyrie Eleison

The response "Lord, have mercy" in Greek is Kyrie, eleison. It is the oldest continuous prayer of the Church, found in the Psalms, on the lips of the blind man on the road to Jericho, and in every Christian liturgy from the earliest centuries to the present.

III
Part Three

The Kathisma

Reading from the Psalter

At Great Vespers on Saturday evenings, the first section of the first kathisma of the Psalter is sung: Blessed is the man who walks not in the counsel of the wicked... This is Psalm 1, which opens the entire Psalter and sets its theme of two ways: the way of blessing and the way of ruin. At Daily Vespers, the appointed kathisma is simply read. At certain great feasts, the kathisma is omitted entirely.

At Great Vespers, the first section of the first kathisma (Blessed is the man...) is sung by the choir. At Daily Vespers, the appointed kathisma is read. The kathisma is omitted on the eves of great feasts of the Lord, except on Saturday or Sunday evening.

The Psalter at Vespers

Blessed is the Man

Psalm 1, the opening of the Psalter

The Orthodox Church divides the Psalter into twenty sections called kathismata. The full Psalter is read through each week in the ordinary course of services, twice a week during Great Lent. To pray at Vespers is to join the continuous, unbroken song of the Church that has never stopped singing the Psalms.

At the conclusion of the kathisma, the deacon or priest says the Little Litany:

Deacon (Priest): Again and again in peace, let us pray to the Lord.

R. Lord, have mercy.

Help us, save us, have mercy on us, and keep us, O God, by Thy grace.

R. Lord, have mercy.

Commemorating our most holy, most pure, most blessed and glorious Lady Theotokos and Ever-virgin Mary with all the saints, let us commend ourselves and each other, and all our life unto Christ our God.

R. To Thee, O Lord.

Priest: For Thine is the might, and Thine is the Kingdom and the power and the glory, of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.

R. Amen.

The Psalter in Orthodox Life

The Psalms are the prayer book of the Old Testament and the backbone of all Orthodox worship. Monks pray the entire Psalter weekly. The faithful at parish services hear large portions of it at every Vespers and Matins throughout the year.

IV
Part Four

Lord, I Call
upon Thee

The evening psalms and the stichera

The choir opens with the verses of Psalm 140 set to music, the ancient evening prayer asking that our prayer rise to God as incense and our lifted hands be as an evening sacrifice. The reader then chants the verses of Psalms 140, 141, 129, and 116, into which the choir inserts the appointed stichera, hymns proper to the feast or day. This is the musical and poetic heart of Vespers, where the Church sings its evening theology.

Choir: Lord, I call upon Thee, hear me! Hear me, O Lord! Lord, I call upon Thee, hear me! Receive the voice of my prayer, when I call upon Thee! Hear me, O Lord!

Let my prayer arise in Thy sight as incense, and let the lifting up of my hands be an evening sacrifice! Hear me, O Lord!

The reader then chants the verses of Psalms 140, 141, 129, and 116. The stichera are inserted between the verses according to the number appointed for the day.

Psalm 140

Set a guard over my mouth, O Lord, keep watch over the door of my lips. Incline not my heart to any evil, to busy myself with wicked deeds in company with men who work iniquity; and let me not eat of their dainties. Let a good man strike or rebuke me in kindness, but let the oil of the wicked never anoint my head; for my prayer is continually against their wicked deeds. When they are given over to those who shall condemn them, then they shall learn that the word of the Lord is true. As a rock which one cleaves and shatters on the land, so shall their bones be strewn at the mouth of Sheol. But my eyes are toward Thee, O Lord God; in Thee I seek refuge; leave me not defenseless. Keep me from the trap which they have laid for me, and from the snares of evildoers. Let the wicked together fall into their own nets, while I escape.

Psalm 141

I cry with my voice to the Lord, with my voice I make supplication to the Lord, I pour out my complaint before Him, I tell my trouble before Him. When my spirit is faint, Thou knowest my way. In the path where I walk they have hidden a trap for me. I look to the right and watch, but there is none who takes notice of me; no refuge remains to me, no man cares for me. I cry to Thee, O Lord; I say, Thou art my refuge, my portion in the land of the living. Give heed to my cry; for I am brought very low. Deliver me from my persecutors; for they are too strong for me.

V. (10) Bring my soul out of prison, that I may give thanks to Thy Name.

V. (9) The righteous will surround me, for Thou wilt deal bountifully with me.

Psalm 129

V. (8) Out of the depths I cry to Thee, O Lord. Lord, hear my voice!

V. (7) Let Thine ears be attentive to the voice of my supplication.

V. (6) If Thou, O Lord, shouldst mark iniquities, Lord, who could stand? But there is forgiveness with Thee.

V. (5) For Thy Name's sake I wait for Thee, O Lord. My soul has waited for Thy word; my soul has hoped on the Lord.

V. (4) From the morning watch until night, from the morning watch, let Israel hope on the Lord.

V. (3) For with the Lord there is mercy, and with Him is plenteous redemption, and He will deliver Israel from all his iniquities.

Psalm 116

V. (2) Praise the Lord, all nations! Praise Him, all peoples!

V. (1) For His mercy is confirmed on us, and the truth of the Lord endures forever.

Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit.
Now and ever and unto ages of ages. Amen.

Incense and Prayer

"Let my prayer arise in Thy sight as incense." As the psalm is chanted, the priest censes the entire church. The fragrant smoke rising upward is a physical sign of prayer ascending to God, a practice reaching back to the Temple in Jerusalem.

V
Part Five

The Evening Entrance
and Gladsome Light

The oldest evening hymn of the Church

At Great Vespers, following the last sticheron, there is a solemn entrance through the Holy Doors: the priest and deacon process with candles and incense as the deacon exclaims "Wisdom. Stand upright." The choir then sings the Gladsome Light, one of the oldest Christian hymns still in use, greeting Christ as the light of evening, the immortal light of the heavenly Father.

If it is Great Vespers, the deacon stands before the open Holy Doors and exclaims: Wisdom. Stand upright. If it is Daily Vespers, there is no entrance, and the choir proceeds immediately to Gladsome Light.

Choir: Gladsome Light of the Holy Glory of the Immortal Father, Heavenly, Holy, Blessed: O Jesus Christ. Now that we have come to the setting of the sun, and behold the light of evening, we praise God: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, for meet it is at all times to worship Thee with voices of praise, O Son of God and Giver of life: therefore, all the world doth glorify Thee.

If it is Great Vespers, the priest and deacon proceed to the high place. The deacon exclaims: Let us attend. The priest: Peace be unto all. The deacon: Wisdom. And they chant the appointed Prokeimenon.

If it is Daily Vespers, the priest stands before the holy table and proclaims the Prokeimenon in the appointed tone.

Gladsome Light

St. Basil the Great, writing in the fourth century, described the singing of Gladsome Light at the lighting of the evening lamps as an ancient custom even in his day. It is among the oldest non-scriptural Christian hymns in continuous use.

VI
Part Six

The Prokeimenon

A psalm verse chanted antiphonally before the readings

The prokeimenon is a verse from the Psalms chanted antiphonally between the priest or deacon and the choir. A different prokeimenon is appointed for each day of the week, each in its proper tone. On great feasts, a special prokeimenon or one of the Great Prokeimena is substituted. It prepares the faithful to receive the scriptural readings that follow at Great Vespers.

Saturday Evening Tone 6

The Lord is King: / He is robed in majesty. (Ps. 92:1a)

V. 1. The Lord is robed, He is girded with strength. (Ps. 92:1b)

V. 2. For He has established the world, so that it shall never be moved. (Ps. 92:2)

V. 3. Holiness befits Thy house, O Lord, forevermore! (Ps. 92:7b)

Sunday Evening Tone 8

Behold now, bless the Lord, / all you servants of the Lord. (Ps. 133:1)

V. You that stand in the house of the Lord, even in the courts of the house of our God. (Ps. 133:2)

Monday Evening Tone 4

The Lord will hear me / when I call upon Him. (Ps. 4:4b)

V. When I called, the God of my righteousness heard me. (Ps. 4:1a)

Tuesday Evening Tone 1

Thy mercy, O Lord, / shall follow me all the days of my life. (Ps. 22:7a)

V. The Lord is my shepherd; therefore can I lack nothing: He maketh me to lie down in a green pasture. (Ps. 22:1-2a)

Wednesday Evening Tone 5

Save me, O God, by Thy Name, / and judge me by Thy strength. (Ps. 53:1)

V. Hear my prayer, O God: hearken unto the words of my mouth. (Ps. 53:2)

Thursday Evening Tone 6

My help comes from the Lord / Who made heaven and earth. (Ps. 120:2)

V. I lifted up mine eyes unto the hills: from whence will my help come? (Ps. 120:1)

Friday Evening Tone 7

Thou, O God, art my helper, / and Thy mercy shall go before me. (Ps. 58:9b-10a)

V. Rescue me from mine enemies, O God: and deliver me from them that rise up against me. (Ps. 58:1)

The Great Prokeimena

Special Occasions

Sung on the eves of certain great feasts

On the eves of Christmas, Thomas Sunday, and Holy Pentecost: Who is so great a god as our God? Thou art the God Who doest wonders. (Ps. 76:13, Tone 7) / On the eves of the Exaltation of the Cross, Theophany, Ascension, and Transfiguration: Our God is in heaven and on earth: He hath done whatsoever He hath pleased. (Ps. 113:11, Tone 7) / On Soul Saturdays: Alleluia. Alleluia. Alleluia. (Tone 8) with verses from Ps. 64 and Ps. 101.

The Eight Tones

Orthodox hymnody is organized into eight musical tones, each with its own character. The tones rotate on an eight-week cycle called the Octoechos, or "Eight Tones," compiled largely by St. John of Damascus in the eighth century.

VII
Part Seven

The Augmented Litany

At Great Vespers, following the Prokeimenon

At Great Vespers, after the prokeimenon and any appointed scriptural lessons, the deacon begins the Augmented Litany. It is "augmented" in fervor: the triple response "Lord, have mercy" replaces the single response of the Great Litany, intensifying the communal plea. It includes commemorations of the departed Orthodox faithful, the living members of the parish, and those who serve in the temple.

At Great Vespers, following the prokeimenon (or during the third lesson when lessons are read), the deacon begins the Augmented Litany. At Daily Vespers this litany is said after the Troparia, before the Dismissal.

Deacon (Priest): Let us say with all our soul and with all our mind, let us say:

R. Lord, have mercy.

O Lord almighty, the God of our fathers, we pray Thee, hearken and have mercy.

R. Lord, have mercy.

Have mercy on us, O God, according to Thy great goodness, we pray Thee, hearken and have mercy.

R. Lord, have mercy. (thrice)

Again we pray for our Metropolitan N., for our Bishop N., and for all our brethren in Christ.

R. Lord, have mercy. (thrice)

Again we pray for this country, its President, for all civil authorities, and for the armed forces.

R. Lord, have mercy. (thrice)

Again we pray for the blessed and ever-memorable holy Orthodox Patriarchs; and for the blessed and ever-memorable founders of this holy church; and for all our fathers and brethren, the Orthodox departed this life before us, who here and in all the world lie asleep in the Lord.

R. Lord, have mercy. (thrice)

Again we pray for mercy, life, peace, health, salvation, and visitation for the servants of God, the brethren of this holy temple, and for the pardon and remission of their sins.

R. Lord, have mercy. (thrice)

Again we pray for those who bring offerings and do good works in this holy and all-venerable temple; for those who labor and those who sing; and for all the people here present, who await Thy great and rich mercy.

R. Lord, have mercy. (thrice)

Priest: For Thou art a merciful God and lovest mankind, and unto Thee do we send up glory: to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.

R. Amen.

Interceding for the Departed

The Orthodox Church prays for the departed at every Vespers, believing that prayer for those who have fallen asleep in the Lord is a work of love that transcends death. The Augmented Litany joins the living and the dead in one act of petition before God.

VIII
Part Eight

Vouchsafe, O Lord
and the Evening Litany

Completing the evening in peace

The reader chants Vouchsafe, O Lord, a prayer that blesses God and asks to be kept from sin through the night. The deacon or priest then leads the Evening Litany, asking for the whole evening to be perfect and holy, for an angel of peace to guard the faithful, and for a Christian ending to life. The congregation responds "Grant this, O Lord" to each petition.

Reader: Vouchsafe, O Lord, to keep us this night without sin. Blessed art Thou, O Lord, God of our fathers, and praised and glorified is Thy Name forever. Amen.

Let Thy mercy be upon us, O Lord, as we have set our hope on Thee. Blessed art Thou, O Lord, teach me Thy statutes. Blessed art Thou, O Master, make me to understand Thy commandments. Blessed art Thou, O Holy One, enlighten me with Thy precepts.

Thy mercy, O Lord, endureth forever: despise not the works of Thy hands. To Thee belongeth worship, to Thee belongeth praise, to Thee belongeth glory: to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages. Amen.

The deacon or priest continues with the Evening Litany:

Deacon (Priest): Let us complete our evening prayer unto the Lord.

R. Lord, have mercy.

Help us, save us, have mercy on us, and keep us, O God, by Thy grace.

R. Lord, have mercy.

That the whole evening may be perfect, holy, peaceful, and sinless, let us ask of the Lord.

R. Grant this, O Lord. (after each petition)

An angel of peace, a faithful guide, a guardian of our souls and bodies, let us ask of the Lord.

Pardon and remission of our sins and transgressions, let us ask of the Lord.

All things that are good and profitable for our souls, and peace for the world, let us ask of the Lord.

That we may complete the remaining time of our life in peace and repentance, let us ask of the Lord.

A Christian ending to our life, painless, blameless, and peaceful, and a good defense before the dread Judgment Seat of Christ, let us ask.

Commemorating our most holy, most pure, most blessed and glorious Lady Theotokos and Ever-virgin Mary with all the saints, let us commend ourselves and each other, and all our life unto Christ our God.

R. To Thee, O Lord.

Priest: For Thou art a good God and lovest mankind, and unto Thee do we send up glory: to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.

R. Amen.

Priest: Peace be unto all.

R. And to thy spirit.

Deacon (Priest): Let us bow our heads unto the Lord.

R. To Thee, O Lord.

Priest: O Lord our God, Who didst bow the heavens and come down for the salvation of mankind: Look upon Thy servants and Thine inheritance; for unto Thee, the fearful Judge Who yet lovest mankind, have Thy servants bowed their heads and submissively inclined their necks, awaiting not help from men but entreating Thy mercy and looking confidently for Thy salvation. Guard them at all times, both during this present evening and in the approaching night, from every foe, from all adverse powers of the devil and from vain thoughts and from evil imaginations.

Blessed and glorified be the might of Thy Kingdom: of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.

R. Amen.

An Angel of Peace

Among the petitions of the Evening Litany is the request for "an angel of peace, a faithful guide, a guardian of our souls and bodies." The Orthodox understanding of creation includes the angelic realm as active participants in the lives of the faithful.

IX
Part Nine

The Aposticha

Stichera with psalm verses sung after the litanies

The choir sings the Aposticha, a set of stichera (hymnographic verses) interspersed with psalm verses appointed for the day. On Saturday evenings at Great Vespers, the verses are from Psalm 92: "The Lord is King, He is robed in majesty." On other days, when no other verses are appointed, verses from Psalm 122 are used. The Aposticha conclude with "Glory to the Father..." and "Now and ever..."

The choir sings the appointed stichera of the Aposticha. At Great Vespers on Saturdays, the following psalm verses are used:

V. The Lord is King: He is robed in majesty. (Ps. 92:1a)

V. For He hath made the world so sure that it shall not be moved. (Ps. 92:2)

V. Holiness becometh Thine house, O Lord, unto length of days. (Ps. 92:7b)

Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit.

Now and ever and unto ages of ages. Amen.

On other days, if no other verses are appointed, the following verses from Psalm 122 are used:

The Hymnographic Tradition

The stichera of the Aposticha are among the most theologically rich hymns in Orthodox worship. They are drawn from the Octoechos, the Menaion (calendar saints), and the Triodion or Pentecostarion during their respective seasons.

Part Ten

Nunc Dimittis
and the Troparia

St. Symeon's canticle and the concluding hymns

After the final sticheron of the Aposticha, St. Symeon's Prayer is chanted: "Lord, now lettest Thou Thy servant depart in peace." These are the words the righteous elder Simeon spoke when he held the Christ child in the Temple. At Vespers, every evening becomes a moment of holding Christ and then being sent out in peace. The Trisagion and Our Father follow, and then the choir sings the appointed Troparia, hymns proper to the feast or the tone of the week.

After the final sticheron of the Aposticha, St. Symeon's Prayer is read or sung by the choir:

The Righteous Simeon

The elder Simeon had been promised by the Holy Spirit that he would not die before seeing the Messiah. When Mary and Joseph brought the infant Jesus to the Temple, Simeon took him in his arms and spoke these words. At Vespers, we become Simeon, holding Christ in prayer and then released in peace.

Part Eleven

The Dismissal

The blessing and the sending forth

At Great Vespers, the Dismissal follows immediately after the Troparia have been sung. At Daily Vespers, the Augmented Litany is said first. The dismissal is a priestly blessing that sends the faithful into the night with the grace of the evening prayer. The priest commemorates Christ, the Theotokos, and the saints of the day, and the choir responds with "Amen."

The End of Vespers

The faithful are dismissed in peace

The evening service ends, but the day of the Lord continues. At Saturday Great Vespers, the Sunday Resurrection has already begun. The faithful depart carrying the grace of the evening prayer into the night, and will return in the morning to complete the celebration with the Divine Liturgy.

Orthodox Church Interior · Evening Service On Evening Prayer
"At the lighting of the evening lamps, the faithful gather to give thanks to God. This is the ancient custom of the Church, and it has never ceased." St. Basil the Great · On the Holy Spirit
Daily Vespers and Great Vespers

Great Vespers is celebrated on Saturday evenings and the eves of great feasts. Daily Vespers is served on other evenings. Both follow the same order, with Great Vespers including the solemn entrance, Old Testament lessons, and an augmented litany.

Church of the Transfiguration · Kizhi Island, Russia The Opening of Vespers
"Bless the Lord, O my soul. O Lord my God, Thou art very great." Psalm 103:1
A Cosmic Hymn

Psalm 103 sings of God governing all creation: the seasons, the creatures, the rising and setting of the sun. As it is chanted, the priest silently reads the Prayers of the Lamp, asking that the evening light be a sign of the light that no darkness can overcome.

Deesis Mosaic · Hagia Sophia, Constantinople, 13th century The Great Litany

The Great Litany prays for the peace of the whole world, for every city and countryside, for travelers, the sick, captives, and all people. No one and nothing is excluded from the Church's intercession at Vespers.

"Have mercy on me, O God, according to Thy steadfast love; according to Thy abundant mercy blot out my transgressions." Psalm 51:1
Rabbula Gospels · Syria, 6th century The Psalter
"Blessed is the man who walks not in the counsel of the wicked, nor stands in the way of sinners, nor sits in the seat of scoffers; but his delight is in the law of the Lord." Psalm 1:1-2 · Opening of the Psalter
Twenty Sections

The Psalter is divided into twenty kathismata. Parishes read through the entire Psalter once each week in the ordinary course of services, and twice weekly during Great Lent.

Iconostasis · Church of Saint Sava, Belgrade Evening Incense
"Let my prayer arise in Thy sight as incense, and let the lifting up of my hands be an evening sacrifice." Psalm 140:2

As these verses are chanted, the priest censes the entire church: the icons, the clergy, and all the faithful. The fragrant smoke rising upward has been the sign of prayer ascending to God from the Temple in Jerusalem to every Orthodox parish in the world.

Royal Doors · Church of the Dormition Gladsome Light
"Now that we have come to the setting of the sun, and behold the light of evening, we praise God: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit." The Hymn of the Gladsome Light · Before 4th century
The Oldest Evening Hymn

St. Basil the Great cited Gladsome Light as a custom of great antiquity even in the fourth century. It greets Christ as the light that does not fade when the sun sets, the eternal light of the Father made visible in the Son.

Greek Orthodox Church Interior · 18th century The Prokeimenon

The prokeimenon is a psalm verse sung antiphonally as a preparation for the scriptural readings that follow at Great Vespers. Its name means "that which is set before" in Greek: a short herald before the lesson.

The Eight Tones

All Orthodox hymnody is organized into eight tones, each with a distinct musical character. The tone of the week rotates on an eight-week cycle called the Octoechos, compiled largely by St. John of Damascus in the eighth century.

Saint Basil the Great · Bishop of Caesarea Triple Mercy
"Lord, have mercy. Lord, have mercy. Lord, have mercy." The threefold cry is not mere repetition. It is the full weight of human need placed before the Trinity.
Praying for the Departed

The Church prays for those who have fallen asleep in the Lord at every service. Orthodox theology holds that prayer for the departed is an act of love that transcends death, and that the living and the dead remain united in the one Body of Christ.

Romanian Orthodox Monastery · Interior Completing the Day
"It is good to give thanks to the Lord, to sing praises to Thy Name, O Most High; to declare Thy steadfast love in the morning, and Thy faithfulness by night." Psalm 92:1-2

The prayer at the bowing of heads asks God to guard the faithful through the approaching night: from every foe, from the powers of darkness, from vain thoughts and evil imaginations. The whole person, soul and body, is commended to God's protection.

Russian Orthodox Iconostasis · Deventer The Hymnographic Tradition

The stichera of the Aposticha are drawn from the Octoechos (eight tones), the Menaion (calendar saints), and the Triodion or Pentecostarion during their respective liturgical seasons. The greatest Orthodox hymnographers, including St. John of Damascus and St. Romanos the Melodist, contributed to this treasury.
