I
Part One

The Litany of Peace
The Great Litany

The opening prayer of the Church
The priest kisses the Holy Gospel, lifts it up with both hands, and lowering it, blesses the altar cross-wise.
Priest Blessed is the Kingdom of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, now and ever unto ages of ages.
People Amen.
The people respond singing "Lord, have mercy" after each petition.
Priest In peace, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For the peace from above and for the salvation of our souls, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For the peace of the whole world, for the welfare of the holy churches of God, and for the union of all, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For this holy house and for those who enter with faith, reverence, and the fear of God, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For His Beatitude, our Metropolitan, Tikhon, for His Eminence, our Archbishop, Michael, for the honorable priesthood, the diaconate in Christ, for all the clergy and the people, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For this country, its president, for all civil authorities, and for the armed forces, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For this city, for every city and countryside, and for the faithful dwelling in them, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For seasonable weather, for abundance of the fruits of the earth, and for peaceful times, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For travelers by land, by sea, and by air; for the sick and the suffering; for captives and their salvation, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For our deliverance from all affliction, wrath, danger, and necessity, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest Help us, save us, have mercy on us, and keep us, O God, by Thy grace.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest Commemorating our most holy, most pure, most blessed and glorious Lady the Theotokos, the ever-virgin Mary, with all the saints, let us commend ourselves and each other, and all our life, unto Christ our God.
People To Thee, O Lord.
Priest For unto Thee are due all glory, honor, and worship: to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.
People Amen.
Kyrie Eleison

The Greek Kyrie, eleison has been the heartbeat of Orthodox prayer since the earliest communities. To pray it at Liturgy is to join every prayer of the Church from the catacombs to the present day.

The Little Litany

The Little Litany

After the First Antiphon The people sing "Lord, have mercy" after each petition.

The Antiphons

In the ancient Church, the antiphons were chanted by the congregation as they processed to the church. Today they open the Liturgy in place of that procession, drawing the faithful inward in prayer.

The Second Little Litany

The Second Little Litany

Priest Again and again in peace let us pray to the Lord.

People Lord, have mercy.
Priest Commemorating our most holy, most pure, most blessed and glorious Lady the Theotokos, the ever-virgin Mary, with all the saints, let us commend ourselves and each other, and all our life unto Christ our God.

Only Begotten Son

This hymn is a masterful summary of Orthodox Christology, composed by the Emperor Justinian in the 6th century. Every phrase responds to a heresy about the nature of Christ.

Part Four

The Third Antiphon
The Beatitudes

Matthew 5:3–12
The people sing while the priest prays silently.
People
In Thy Kingdom remember us, O Lord,
    when Thou comest in Thy Kingdom.
Blessed are the poor in spirit,
    for theirs is the Kingdom of Heaven.
Blessed are those who mourn,
    for they shall be comforted.
Blessed are the meek,
    for they shall inherit the earth.
Blessed are those who hunger and thirst
    after righteousness, for they shall be filled.
Blessed are the merciful,
    for they shall obtain mercy.
Blessed are the pure in heart,
    for they shall see God.
Blessed are the peacemakers,
    for they shall be called the sons of God.
Blessed are those who are persecuted
    for righteousness' sake, for theirs
    is the Kingdom of Heaven.
Blessed are you when men shall revile you
    and persecute you, and shall say
    all manner of evil against you falsely
    for my sake.
Rejoice and be exceedingly glad,
    for great is your reward in heaven.
The Beatitudes

The Beatitudes summarize the entire Sermon on the Mount and the life of the Kingdom. Sung at this moment, they prepare the faithful for the entrance of the Gospel into the church.

V
Part Five

The Little Entrance

The entrance of the Holy Gospel
Priest Wisdom! Let us attend!
The celebrants enter the sanctuary while the people sing the Entrance Hymn.
People
Come, let us worship and fall down before Christ,
Who art risen from the dead,
O Son of God, save us who sing to Thee Alleluia!
(On Weekdays:) Who is Wonderful in His Saints,
(On Feasts of the Theotokos:) Through the prayers of the Theotokos,
The Little Entrance

Originally this was the entrance of the bishop and clergy into the church to begin the service. The Gospel book carried in procession represents Christ Himself entering among His people.

VI
Part Six

Troparia & Kontakia
The Trisagion

Holy God, Holy Mighty, Holy Immortal
While the priest prays silently, the Troparia and Kontakia of the liturgical day are sung by the choir. After they have been sung, the priest prays aloud:
Priest For holy art Thou, O our God, and unto Thee we ascribe glory: to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.
Choir Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto the ages of ages. Amen.
The Trisagion

The Trisagion

The thrice-holy hymn of the angels
People
Holy God! Holy Mighty! Holy Immortal!
Have mercy on us. (3x)
Glory to the Father, and to the Son, and to the Holy Spirit,
now and ever and unto ages of ages. Amen.

Holy Immortal! Have mercy on us.
Holy God! Holy Mighty! Holy Immortal! Have mercy on us.
People Amen.
Trisagion

The Trisagion, "thrice-holy," echoes the song of the Seraphim from Isaiah 6 and the vision of Revelation 4. The Church joins the angels in their eternal worship at this moment.

The Homily

The Homily

The people are invited to sit if the Homily is given. Upon completion, the people stand and prepare to sing the Litany of Fervent Supplication.
The Word of God

The Old and New Testament scriptures were originally read almost exclusively as a communal act. The services of the church remained the primary opportunity to encounter scripture for many centuries of Christian life.

The Litany for the Departed

The Litany for the Departed

Served except on Sundays and Feast Days
Note

The Litany for the Departed may be recited here, except on Sundays and Feast Days.

Priest Again we pray for the blessed and ever-memorable holy Orthodox patriarchs; and for the blessed and ever-memorable founders of this holy church _______________; and for all our fathers and brethren, the Orthodox departed this life before us, who here and in all the world lie asleep in the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy. (3x)
Intensified Supplication

Where the Great Litany responds once, this litany responds three times. The triple "Lord, have mercy" expresses deeper urgency, calling on God not as a formality, but with the full weight of the community's need.

Priest All catechumens, depart. Depart, catechumens. All that are catechumens, depart. Let no catechumen remain.
An Ancient Practice

In earlier centuries the dismissal of catechumens was a solemn and literal act. The doors of the church were closed and guarded. The Liturgy that followed was considered so sacred that it was not to be witnessed by those not yet baptized.

The Threshold

With the catechumens departed, these two short litanies draw the congregation into final preparation for the Eucharistic offering. They mark the solemn boundary between the Liturgy of the Word and the Liturgy of the Faithful.

XI
Part Eleven

The Cherubic Hymn
The Great Entrance

The procession of the Holy Gifts to the altar
At This Time

An offering plate is passed to the people. When the bread and wine are offered to God to be sanctified, the people in turn give their own offering.

The first part of the Cherubic Hymn is sung while the Priest censes the people.
People
Let us who mystically represent the Cherubim,
and who sing the thrice-holy hymn
to the life-creating Trinity,
now lay aside all earthly cares.
When the Priest enters the Sanctuary, the people continue singing the Cherubic Hymn. Before returning the bread and wine to the altar, the priest asks Christ to remember in His kingdom various groups of people. The last of these petitions is:
Priest You, and all Orthodox Christians, may the Lord God remember in His Kingdom, always, now and ever, and unto ages of ages.
People Amen.
People
That we may receive the King of all,
Who comes invisibly upborne by the angelic hosts.
Alleluia! Alleluia! Alleluia!
The Great Entrance

The Great Entrance mirrors the journey of Christ to His voluntary sacrifice. The bread and wine, now carried to the altar, will become His Body and Blood. We receive the King of all.

People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For this holy house, and for those who enter it with faith, reverence, and the fear of God, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest That we may be delivered from all affliction, wrath, danger and necessity, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest Help us, save us, have mercy on us, and keep us, O God, by Thy grace.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest That the whole day may be perfect, holy, peaceful, and sinless, let us ask of the Lord.
People Grant it, O Lord.
Priest An angel of peace, a faithful guide, a guardian of our souls and bodies, let us ask of the Lord.
People Grant it, O Lord.
Priest Pardon and remission of our sins and transgressions, let us ask of the Lord.
People Grant it, O Lord.
Priest All things that are good and profitable for our souls, and peace for the world, let us ask of the Lord.
People Grant it, O Lord.
Priest That we may complete the remaining time of our life in peace and repentance, let us ask of the Lord.
People Grant it, O Lord.
Priest A Christian ending to our life: painless, blameless, and peaceful; and a good defense before the dread judgement seat of Christ, let us ask of the Lord.
People Grant it, O Lord.
Priest Commemorating our most holy, most pure, most blessed and glorious Lady, the Theotokos, the ever-virgin Mary, with all the saints, let us commend ourselves and each other, and all our life unto Christ our God.
People To Thee, O Lord.
Priest Through the compassions of Thine only-begotten Son, with whom Thou art blessed, together with Thine all-holy, good, and life-creating Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.
People Amen.
Grant It, O Lord

The shift from "Lord, have mercy" to "Grant it, O Lord" signals a change in tone. The litanies of supplication ask not only for mercy but for specific gifts: peace, an angel of peace, a Christian ending to our life.

The Creed

The Creed, The Symbol of Our Faith

Priest The doors. The doors! In wisdom, let us attend!
The people and priest sing together The Nicene Creed.
Priest And with thy spirit.
All
I believe in one God,
    the Father Almighty,
    Maker of Heaven and Earth
    and of all things visible and invisible.

And in one Lord Jesus Christ,
    the Son of God,
    the only-begotten,
    begotten of the Father before all ages.
        Light of light;
        true God of true God;
    begotten, not made; of
    one essence with the Father;
    by Whom all things were made;
    Who for us men and for our salvation
        came down from Heaven,
        and was incarnate of the Holy Spirit and the Virgin Mary,
        and became man.
        And He was crucified for us under Pontius Pilate,
        and suffered, and was buried.
        And the third day He arose again, according to the Scriptures,

    I look for the resurrection of the dead
    and the life of the world to come.
Amen.
The Nicene Creed

Formulated at the Council of Nicaea in 325 AD and completed at Constantinople in 381, the Creed is the universal confession of the Orthodox Church. All who believe stand and sing it together.

XIV
Part Fourteen

The Anaphora

The Eucharistic prayer of thanksgiving and consecration
Exhorting the people to ready themselves for the eucharistic offering, the priest exclaims:
Priest Let us stand aright! Let us stand with fear! Let us attend, that we may offer the Holy Oblation in peace.
People A mercy of peace! A sacrifice of praise!
Priest The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, the love of God the Father, and the communion of the Holy Spirit be with you all.
People And with thy spirit.
Priest Let us lift up our hearts.
People We lift them up unto the Lord.
Priest Let us give thanks unto the Lord.
People It is meet and right to worship the Father, and the Son, and the Holy Spirit: the Trinity, one in essence, and undivided.
Singing the triumphant hymn, shouting, proclaiming and saying:
People Holy! Holy! Holy! Lord of Sabaoth! Heaven and earth are full of Thy glory! Hosanna in the highest! Blessed is He that comes in the name of the Lord! Hosanna in the highest!
Priest Take! Eat! This is My Body which is broken for you, for the remission of sins.
People Amen.
Priest Drink of it, all of you! This is My Blood of the New Covenant, which is shed for you and for many, for the remission of sins.
People Amen.
Priest Offering unto Thee Thine own of Thine own on behalf of all and for all.
People Amen.
The priest continues praying silently, while the people sing:
People We praise Thee. We bless Thee. We give thanks unto Thee, O Lord. And we pray unto Thee, O our God.
The Hymn to the Theotokos

The Hymn to the Theotokos

It is truly meet
The priest silently commemorates the martyrs, ascetics, prophets, and all the saints. Finally he commemorates Mary, the Theotokos:
Priest Especially for our most holy, most pure, most blessed and glorious Lady the Theotokos the ever-virgin Mary.
People
It is truly meet to bless thee, O Theotokos,
ever-blessed and most pure, and the
Mother of our God.
More honorable than the Cherubim, and
more glorious beyond compare than the Seraphim:
Without corruption thou gavest birth to
God the Word:
True Theotokos, we magnify thee.
All Mankind

All Mankind

The priest prays for the hierarchs of the Church, and the people respond asking God to remember all people in His mercy.
Priest Among the first, remember, O Lord, His Beatitude, our Metropolitan Tikhon; His Eminence, our Archbishop Michael. Grant them for Thy holy churches in peace, safety, honor, health, and length of days, rightly to divide the word of Thy truth.
People And all mankind.
Priest And may the mercies of our great God and Savior Jesus Christ be with you all.
People And with thy spirit.
Priest And grant that with one mouth and one heart we may praise Thine all-honorable and majestic name: of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, now and ever and unto ages of ages.
People Amen.
The Anaphora

The Anaphora, meaning "offering," is the central prayer of the Eucharist. The words of institution from the Last Supper are spoken, and the Holy Spirit is called upon to transform the bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ.

XV
Part Fifteen

Litany Before
the Lord's Prayer

Preparing to approach the Holy Gifts
The people respond singing "Lord, have mercy" after each petition.
Priest Having remembered all the saints, again and again in peace let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.
Priest For the precious Gifts now offered and sanctified, let us pray to the Lord.
People Lord, have mercy.

Notes by Fr. Solomon Longo

Below are some guidelines on etiquette. Do not worry about getting them wrong at times.

Participation

What should I be doing during the Liturgy?

Instructions on how to participate in the Divine Liturgy

When do I stand? If the priest is outside the doors to the altar, standing is called for unless the homily is in progress or if prayers are being said while kneeling. Standing is almost always appropriate; pews are a recent occurrence in Orthodox services; pews are useful but should not be overused. Especially stand during the Gospel reading, the Little and Great Entrances, the Creed, the Anaphora, the Lord's Prayer and while Communion is distributed. When sitting, one should remain reverent. We have saintly examples of worshipping peacefully, reverently, simply, joyfully, and tearfully, but no examples of worshipping casually.

What about clothing? The worship of God should ultimately come to evoke an attitude that is neither casual nor immodest and pretentious. Traditionally, men are not to cover their heads, and women are to wear head coverings. Shoes may be removed in accordance with some Orthodox traditions. At no time will a visitor be criticized for their clothing; it is up to the person to conscientiously choose their attire.

Who can receive holy communion? Only those received into the Orthodox Church by baptism and/or chrismation. Additionally, the faithful should confess at the frequency agreed upon with the priest in order to receive. The ancient churches have never distributed communion incautiously, as it is sacred.

What do we do with our children? Children become full members in the Orthodox Church, receiving baptism, chrismation, and communion. As such, they should grow in capacity to attend the whole service. Screens should not be used to pacify. Soft toys, drawing, and books may be helpful at times. Children having an unusually difficult time may be taken out for a break but please bring them back in soon, as being in the service should be normative and is necessary for them to become calmer and more attentive over time.

Embodied Prayer

What is the Sign of the Cross?

The Sign of the Cross is of apostolic origin. Early Christian writers of the 200s AD, such as Tertullian and St. Cyprian speak of its antiquity. Tertullian wrote about its use in daily life: "when we come in or go out, when we dress, when we wash." The gesture involves tracing a cross on one's body, from the forehead to the chest, then from the right shoulder to the left shoulder.

When people make the Sign of the Cross they are "blessing themselves." This is a physical prayer. The body prays and worships, too. The body, and physical world matter. Faith is not limited to our thoughts. As taught since the time of the earliest Christians, making the sign of the cross is a powerful symbol of protection by unity with Christ.

How the Liturgy Reflects Reality

Why are people moving around?

Apart from the young children, adults also move around during the service. You may see people venerate icons or light candles during the service. People usually do this when they arrive, even if they are late. When the priest addresses the people, or at sacred times like the Great Entrance or Gospel reading, people remain still; at other times it is normal to occasionally go about one's prayers within the ongoing prayer of the community as a whole. Visitors are always welcome to light candles and venerate icons.

Manifesting Prayer into the Material World

Why all the candles and incense?

The Church is pre-modern and manifests a nearly timeless life within the Holy Spirit. Candles are not only for beauty, but also join a physical, olfactory and visual aspect to prayer. People placing candles are giving small gifts of beauty to God while also "materializing" their prayer. Because of the multi-faceted meaning of the candles, they have been lit in worship since the earliest years of the church, even in the daytime. Like candles, incense is present from Old Testament times, and in accordance with the book of Revelation. Incense is offered by the priest with the sign of the cross and the prayer, "We offer Thee incense, O Christ our God, as a sweet smelling spiritual fragrance, receive it upon Thy heavenly altar and send down upon us, in return, the grace of Thy Holy Spirit."

Sing and Make Music in Your Hearts to the Lord

What should I say during the Liturgy?

The Orthodox Church has historically valued the languages and music of multiple cultures. Our services are in English, but you may occasionally hear a bit of Greek and Slavonic for the Trisagion or the Cherubic Hymn.

The predominant use of English allows many people to participate, and the Liturgy is indeed meant to be truly prayed by everyone in attendance. This begins with attention. St. Theophan the Recluse says that prayers without attention are like letters without addresses. We should mean the lines of the liturgy and make them our true prayers.

When singing along with the choir and congregation, sing what you've learned, chant what you've heard enough times to remember. The services are internalized over the course of time. Do not feel as though you are supposed to understand or remember every prayer immediately.

Christ Pantocrator icon What Is the Divine Liturgy?
"The services of the church offer the experience, rare in modern America, of entering into something holy and healing, and which does not change to win us over, but changes us for union with the Highest."
The Heart of Christian Worship

The Divine Liturgy is one of the most ancient and sacred Christian traditions, originating in the earliest days of the Church after the descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost. Rooted in the Apostolic era, this service embodies the central mystery of the Christian faith: the Eucharist, where bread and wine are transfigured to be the Body and Blood of Jesus Christ.

With its beginnings in upper rooms and catacombs, the Liturgy was refined over centuries while preserving its deep spiritual meaning and communal significance. The Liturgy is the high point of the daily cycle of services, which is completed most fully at monasteries.

Deesis mosaic The Great Litany & Antiphons
What Is a Litany?

The word Litany literally means prayer or supplication. Litanies are a form of corporate prayer, uniting the faithful in supplication and invoking God's mercy and grace. They reflect the Liturgy's emphasis on communal worship and intercession for all creation.

What Are Antiphons?

The word Antiphon literally means "sounding against" or "responsive voice." Antiphons are hymns and psalms sung responsively, alternating between two choirs or between choir and congregation, using scriptural verses with refrains. The psalms used vary depending on the feast or season being celebrated.

"Lord, have mercy."The Faithful's Response throughout the Liturgy
Illuminated Byzantine psalter The Music of the Divine Liturgy
The Octoechos

The Octoechos system is an eight-tone musical framework used in Byzantine and Slavic chant to organize specific melodies according to the liturgical calendar. The Temple had a choir of Levites, and the synagogues also made use of the Psalms. Orthodox Christians continued this musical tradition.

Hymnographers like St. Romanos the Melodist and St. John of Damascus composed songs beloved to this day. This was the blossoming of the Byzantine musical tradition, including the Znamenny chants of the Slavic peoples, and the harmonized choral settings that followed, all preserve deep theological richness, connecting American congregations to the ancient musical heritages of the faith.

Byzantine imperial mosaic The Second Antiphon
Only Begotten Son

This hymn was composed by Emperor Justinian I in the 6th century and has been sung at every Divine Liturgy since. Every phrase is a precise theological statement responding to a specific heresy about the nature of Christ.

"O Christ our God, trampling down death by death, save us."Only Begotten Son Hymn
The Sermon on the Mount The Beatitudes

The Beatitudes are not a list of rewards but a description of the Kingdom of Heaven as it exists now, present in those who mourn, the meek, the merciful, and the pure in heart. Sung at this moment, they prepare the faithful for the entrance of the Gospel into the church.

"Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God."Matthew 5:8
Icon of the Theotokos The Little Entrance

The Little Entrance is a solemn procession where the Gospel Book is carried to the altar, symbolizing Christ's entry into the world and His presence among His people. This moment marks the transition to the proclamation of God's Word.

In the past, the antiphons were chanted by the congregation as they processed to the church. The Little Entrance brought the Gospel into the Church to begin the Liturgy itself. The Old and New Testament scriptures were originally read almost exclusively as a communal act, and the services of the church remained the primary opportunity to learn scripture for many centuries.

The Isaiah Scroll Why Is There Chanting?
An Ancient Sacred Practice

Chanting Scripture, such as the Psalms and other readings, has been a practice since ancient Jewish worship, adopted by early Christians and continued in Orthodox liturgy for over two millennia. This melodic proclamation elevates the Word of God, ensuring clarity, creating a prayerful atmosphere, and utilizing at the highest level the God-created instrument: the human voice.

Chanting reflects the heavenly worship described in Revelation 4:8–11, aids memorization, and distinguishes liturgical roles.

Illuminated Gospel manuscript The Proclamation of Scripture
What Is the Prokeimenon?

The word prokeimenon from the Greek literally means "that which precedes." It is a liturgical verse from the Psalms sung responsorially before the Epistle or Gospel. The Prokeimenon of the liturgical day is chanted in one of the 8 liturgical musical tones.

What Is a Homily?

A homily, from Greek homilia literally meaning "discourse," is a sermon by a bishop, priest, or deacon, reflecting on the day's readings, saints, or commemorated events. It applies the Word of God to the life of the congregation.

"The Liturgy may challenge unformed attention spans; this is one of its blessings."On the tradition of attentiveness
Anastasis icon Fervent Supplication
The Triple Response

Where the Great Litany responds once with "Lord, have mercy," the Litany of Fervent Supplication responds three times. The tripling is an ancient intensification of petition, expressing that the congregation calls on God not as a formality, but with the full weight of their need.

"Lord, have mercy. Lord, have mercy. Lord, have mercy."The Triple Response of the Litany
Baptism of Christ mosaic Who Are the Catechumens?
Those Being Instructed

Catechumens from the Greek katechoumenos, meaning "one being instructed," are individuals preparing for baptism and full initiation into the Church. The Catechumenate was historically often 3 years in length, during which converts learned to conform their life to the way of Christ.

Historically, catechumens left the sanctuary during the Liturgy for further instruction, as only fully initiated members could remain for the Eucharist. A pre-communion prayer still echoes this caution: "I will not reveal Thy mysteries to Thine enemies, O Lord, nor, like Judas, give Thee a kiss."

Icon of the Holy Trinity Litanies of the Faithful

With the catechumens departed, the Church stands as one body: the baptized, the chrismated, the communing faithful. These two short litanies ask God to cleanse their souls and bodies and make them worthy to offer the Holy Gifts. They mark the solemn boundary between the Liturgy of the Word and the Liturgy of the Faithful.

"For unto Thee are due all glory, honor, and worship."The Closing Doxology
Communion of the Apostles icon The Great Entrance
The Cherubim

Cherubim are angelic throne guardians with four wings and four faces: a human, lion, ox, and eagle. The Cherubic Hymn expresses the unseen reality of the faithful who take their place among the angels, bearing up in their hands our life-creating God Who is being carried to the tomb.

The Great Entrance is a procession in which the bread and wine, prepared during the Prothesis, are carried to the altar. This act mirrors the journey of Christ to His voluntary sacrifice.

Orthodox altar Litany of Supplication
Grant It, O Lord

The shift from "Lord, have mercy" to "Grant it, O Lord" marks a deepening of the Church's prayer. She now asks for specific gifts: peace, an angel of peace, a Christian ending to life, a good defense before the judgment seat. Each petition is an act of trust in God's providence as the gifts rest upon the altar.

The Council of Nicaea The Symbol of Our Faith
What Is a Creed?

The word creed comes from the Latin credo, "I believe." In the Church it is called The Symbol of Faith, meaning literally the "bringing together" and "expression" of the faith. The Creed was established at the Council of Nicaea I (325 AD) and the Council of Constantinople I (381 AD).

Nothing new was declared, but the faith of the persecuted Church was defined over and against heretical beliefs about Christ and the Trinity. The formerly tortured leaders of the Church gathered freely to produce a universal creed giving a secure, trustworthy embodiment to the mysteries of God's self-revelation.

Communion of the Apostles The Anaphora
Mary and All the Saints

Veneration from the Greek word doulia is the act of profound respect and honor to those who have demonstrated extraordinary devotion to Christ. God is the source of all good, all grace, all power. We also know, as a core truth of the Gospel, that God is in His saints, and that Scripture refers to the saints united to Christ as rulers and co-heirs with Him and members of His body.

The Anaphora is the "lifting up" of God's gifts to Him to be sanctified as the Body and Blood of Christ. When the Divine Liturgy is celebrated on a day other than a Great Feast or Sunday, this is among the parts where all kneel and bow their heads to the floor.

The Holy Chalice What Is Happening Before Communion?
The Koinonikon

The Koinonikon is a hymn sung before the faithful prepare to receive Holy Communion. Its name derives from the Greek word koinonia, meaning "communion" or "fellowship." After the Lord's Prayer, the priest prayerfully prepares the Lamb, the bread that has been sanctified to be the Body of Christ, cutting it to be placed in the chalice.

The priest takes great pains that not even the smallest crumb is misplaced or discarded, as it contains the divine Body and Blood of God.

The Lord's Prayer The Lord's Prayer
The Prayer of Christ

The Lord's Prayer, given by Christ in Matthew 6, is sung by the whole congregation immediately before Communion. We approach the chalice as those whom Christ has made worthy to call God "Father." Every petition of this prayer is fulfilled in the Eucharist.

"One is Holy. One is the Lord: Jesus Christ, to the glory of God the Father."The People's Response before Communion
Christ Pantocrator icon Why Is Communion So Important?

St. Ignatius, the martyred 1st century bishop of Antioch, writes: "the Eucharist is the flesh of our Savior Jesus Christ, flesh which suffered for our sins and which the Father, in His goodness, raised up again." St. Justin the Martyr wrote in 150 AD: "this food is called among us Eucharist, of which no one is allowed to partake but the man who believes... and who has been washed with the washing that is for the remission of sins."

Only baptized, practicing Orthodox Christians who have prepared through prayer and fasting receive Communion. All others are warmly welcome to come forward to receive a blessing from the priest.

Hands in prayer Saint John Chrysostom
The Golden-Mouthed

Born in Antioch in the 4th century, St. John Chrysostom was renowned for holiness, his sermons, and spiritual insights. He came to be called "Chrysostom," meaning "Golden-Mouthed." His recension of the Liturgy became the most frequently used in the Orthodox Church.

Saint Basil the Great

Saint Basil the Great (c. 329–379 AD) was an influential Church Father and bishop of Caesarea. His Liturgy was compiled and reformed during his episcopate in the fourth century, evolving from earlier apostolic traditions. The Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom is by far the most frequently celebrated in the Eastern Orthodox Church.
