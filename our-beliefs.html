The Orthodox Christian Faith

Ancient, unchanging, and fully alive

Orthodox Christianity is not a branch of Western Christianity that grew in a different direction. It is the original tree — the undivided Church of the first thousand years, the Faith delivered by Christ to the Apostles, preserved in unbroken continuity through councils, fathers, martyrs, and ordinary believers across twenty centuries.

We do not describe ourselves as the "true Church" in a triumphalist sense. We describe ourselves as the Church that has kept the fullness of the Faith — the Trinitarian theology settled at Nicaea, the Christology clarified at Chalcedon, the liturgical and sacramental life that the early Church recognized as essential to Christian existence.

Core Beliefs

The Holy Trinity
We believe in one God — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — three Persons sharing one divine nature. This is not a mathematical puzzle but the very name of the God who has revealed Himself to us: the Father who creates, the Son who redeems, the Spirit who sanctifies.
Jesus Christ — Fully God and Fully Human
The Second Person of the Trinity took on human flesh, was born of the Virgin Mary, lived a human life, was crucified, died, was buried, and on the third day rose from the dead. He ascended to the Father and will come again to judge the living and the dead. His resurrection is not a metaphor — it is the central fact of history.
The Church
The Church is the Body of Christ — not a voluntary association of like-minded believers, but an organism that participates in the divine life. Membership in the Church is not merely institutional; it is mystical, eucharistic, and transformative.
The Holy Mysteries (Sacraments)
The Orthodox Church recognizes seven Holy Mysteries as the primary means of our participation in the life of God: Baptism, Chrismation, the Eucharist, Repentance (Confession), Holy Orders, Marriage, and Holy Unction. These are not symbolic acts — they are real encounters with the living God.
Scripture and Tradition
Holy Scripture and Holy Tradition are not two separate sources of authority — they are one living stream. Scripture was written within the Church, is interpreted by the Church, and cannot be rightly understood apart from the context of the community that received, preserved, and lives it.
The Theotokos and the Saints
The Virgin Mary — the Theotokos, or God-bearer — holds a unique place in Orthodox piety. We also honor the saints as members of the Church who have passed through death into the nearer presence of God, and who intercede for us before the Throne of grace.

The Nicene Creed

Recited together at every Divine Liturgy since the Fourth Century

I believe in one God, the Father Almighty, Maker of heaven and earth, and of all things visible and invisible.

And in one Lord Jesus Christ, the Son of God, the only-begotten, born of the Father before all ages; Light of Light, true God of true God; begotten, not made; of one essence with the Father, by whom all things were made; who for us men and for our salvation came down from the heavens, and was incarnate of the Holy Spirit and the Virgin Mary, and became man.

And He was crucified for us under Pontius Pilate, and suffered and was buried; and He rose again on the third day, according to the Scriptures; and ascended into the heavens, and is seated at the right hand of the Father; and He shall come again with glory to judge the living and the dead; whose kingdom shall have no end.

And in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the Giver of Life; who proceeds from the Father; who together with the Father and the Son is worshiped and glorified; who spoke by the prophets.

In one, holy, catholic, and apostolic Church. I confess one baptism for the forgiveness of sins. I look for the resurrection of the dead, and the life of the age to come. Amen.

The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed · AD 381
