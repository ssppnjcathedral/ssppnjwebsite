Church History

Two Centuries
on Sacred Ground

A history of faith, architecture, and community on Grand Street since 1804

Overview

Saints Peter and Paul Russian Orthodox Church on Grand Street, founded in 1907, is the second oldest Slavic Orthodox Church in New Jersey. Its congregants — descendants of Eastern European immigrants — belong to the self-governing body of the Orthodox Church of America, operating under the Diocese of New York and New Jersey. The building itself stands as a Contributing Resource of the Paulus Hook Historic District, listed on both the State and National Register of Historic Places since 1982.

For over 200 years, the church site on Grand Street has been reserved exclusively for religious observance. It was one of four lots so designated before any significant settlement took hold in what is now Jersey City's historic district — a testament to the deep roots of faith that have anchored this corner of Paulus Hook since the city's earliest days.

Historic Designation

Saints Peter & Paul Orthodox Cathedral is a Contributing Resource of the Paulus Hook Historic District on the State and National Register of Historic Places (1982), and received matching grants from the New Jersey Historic Trust for its landmark restoration.

The sandstone Gothic Revival façade of Saints Peter & Paul, 109 Grand Street, Jersey City — a Contributing Resource of the Paulus Hook Historic District since 1982.

Paulus Hook Origins

The provenance of the church lot dates to the Associates of the Jersey Company, a development corporation formed for Paulus Hook in 1804. Its leading investors — New York Federalists Anthony Dey, Jacob Radcliffe, Richard Varick, and their attorney Alexander Hamilton, among others — were merchants and lawyers intent on transforming the farms and marshlands of Paulus Hook into a thriving community. They leased the swampy, frontier-like Hudson River waterfront property from Cornelius Van Vorst (1728–1818), a descendant of the site's early seventeenth-century Dutch proprietor.

The Jersey Company commissioned surveyor Joseph F. Mangin to map the tract, dividing it into 200-by-400-foot blocks and subdividing those into 1,344 lots of 25-by-100 feet — most of which were underwater. One thousand shares were offered to prospective buyers to raise revenue for the project.

Joseph F. Mangin's survey of Paulus Hook, 1804, showing the four lots designated "church grounds" — the only sites named on the original map.

Cornelius Van Vorst (1728–1818), descendant of Paulus Hook's early Dutch proprietors, leased the waterfront tract to the Associates of the Jersey Company in 1804.

Among the incentives to attract settlement was a striking designation: at the highest and driest point of the survey, four lots were marked "church grounds" — the only sites named on the original map. As historian Barbara Petrick explains, the original proprietors assumed it was the churches that would provide the anchor of civilization, bringing schools and the formation of decent citizens in their wake.

Four churches were eventually established on the donated properties, though the area's population grew more slowly than the Associates had anticipated. St. Matthew's Protestant Episcopal Church, organized in 1808, was the first to take advantage of the offer, installing its cornerstone in 1831. Saint Peter's Roman Catholic Church began that same year on Grand Street, and the Methodists built a brick church on York Street by 1843.

Church Development on Grand Street

In 1807, the Associates approached members of the Dutch Reformed Church at Bergen Hill to build on the south side of Grand Street. The offer was declined for want of sufficient male members to form a Consistory. The Associates then offered the lot to Presbyterians, who in 1826 laid a cornerstone for a wooden church — the First Presbyterian Church — capable of accommodating 400 parishioners.

The New York Times described the Grand Street property at the time: the ground was all swamp, and the church stood upon 500 piles. Flooding repeatedly threatened the structure, and Dutch farmers of the district reportedly saved it on at least one occasion by hauling fresh earth to bury the exposed piles beneath their carts.

The First Presbyterian Church on Grand Street was short-lived. A change of church affiliation in 1830 converted the congregation to Dutch Reformed. The Grand Street Church, as it was commonly known, never completed the stone structure required under the Associates' agreement, and struggled with unstable leadership and finances for years. A separate First Presbyterian Church was eventually established in 1844 at Sussex and Washington Streets.

On September 22, 1853, the Dutch Reformed Church installed a cornerstone on Grand Street for a cut-stone Gothic Revival church to replace the wooden structure — built on a lot then adjacent to Lyceum Hall, which had served as the first Hudson County courthouse until 1843. Among the items placed in the cornerstone's cavity were the psalms of the Reformed Dutch Church and its constitution.

The first architect engaged for the project was Scottish-born John W. Welch (1824–1894) of Brooklyn, known for the landmark St. Luke's Protestant Episcopal Church in his hometown and Eberhardt Hall at what is now the New Jersey Institute of Technology. A dispute with Welch and cost overages of more than the intended $20,000 left the building with only its outer walls and required the engagement of a new architect.

The New York Times identified the new architect as "Mr. M. Lineau, of Jersey City" — in all likelihood Detlef Lienau (1818–1887), born in Denmark's region that later became part of Germany. Trained in Germany and Paris, Lienau immigrated to America to join his brother Michael in Jersey City, becoming one of the founders of the American Institute of Architects. His Jersey City work included Grace Church Van Vorst at 39 Erie Street (1850), the Mechanics and Traders Bank Building (1859), the original First National Bank building at One Exchange Place (1864), and the American Sugar Refinery at 174 Washington Street (1863).

The completed Dutch Reformed Church, dedicated April 5, 1857, was described by the Times as built of brown sandstone from Belleville and Little Falls in "perpendicular Gothic of the Twentieth Century." Its nave stretched 48 feet high and 30 feet wide beneath an open-timbered roof. The 106-by-70-foot structure held ten stained-glass windows, 128 pews for 750 persons, gas lights, and ten arches on eight columns. Final costs doubled the original estimate, making it the most expensive church then standing in Jersey City.

The original Gothic Revival interior as completed by architect Detlef Lienau in 1857 — ten arches on eight columns, open-timbered roof, and ten stained-glass windows for a congregation of 750.

"Images or icons are silent sermons uttered in color. A wall of icons, the iconostasis, displays saints including Saint Nicholas, a fourth century bishop. Beyond the wall is the sacred inner space of the altar, or Holy of Holies — richly encrusted icons creating an inspiring, worshipful environment."

— Rev. Joseph Lickwar, Pastor of Saints Peter and Paul since 1991
Jersey City's Sacred Spaces, 2004

Saints Peter and Paul on Grand Street

By the 1870s, Jersey City's population surged with the forces of immigration, urbanization, and industrialization. Political consolidation in 1870, the presence of three major railway lines, and employment at Colgate, the Great Atlantic & Pacific Tea Company, P. Lorillard Tobacco, American Sugar Refining, and Dixon Crucible, among other industries, drew newcomers to the city. The population climbed from 82,000 to 200,000 by 1900 — a quantum leap from the handful of residents the Associates had found at Paulus Hook a century earlier.

Eastern European immigrants from Slovakia, Ukraine, and the Russian Empire flooded into Jersey City in the late nineteenth century, drawn by work at the city's railroads and refineries. By 1900 the population had reached 200,000.

Among the new arrivals were Eastern Europeans from what is now Slovakia and Ukraine, former sections of Austria-Hungary, and the Russian Empire. They organized the Saints Peter and Paul Brotherhood under a December 1889 charter, initially holding services in a wooden building on Chestnut Street. By 1902, the congregation had moved into a newly constructed brick church at the southeast corner of Greene and Sussex Streets.

These congregants worshipped as Eastern Rite Catholics — following the Byzantine liturgical rite under the general jurisdiction of Rome. When attempts were made to replace the Byzantine rite with more Latinized forms of worship, a faction of approximately 500 congregants withdrew to form a separate community where their Eastern liturgical traditions could be preserved. They were drawn to the Russian Orthodox movement in North America, led from 1892 by the Very Rev. Alexis Toth.

The seceding faction held services at 74 Grand Street, the former annex of Public School No. 1, and later in "house churches" — first at 131 Grand Street, then at 64 Grand Street, rented for $35.00 monthly. On February 16, 1907, the congregation incorporated as "Saints Peter and Paul Russian Orthodox Greek Catholic Church of Jersey City."

The new congregation petitioned Archbishop Platon of New York to join the Russian Orthodox Greek Catholic Church of America. He responded by assigning Archpriest Alexander Hotovitsky — a founder of the Orthodox presence on Grand Street — who led the parish's First Divine Liturgy at his 64 Grand Street residence on October 24, 1907. In 1994, Rev. Hotovitzky was canonized as a missionary to North America and New Martyr of Russia, for his martyrdom in Russia in 1938.

Archpriest Alexander Hotovitsky led the First Divine Liturgy at 64 Grand Street on October 24, 1907. Canonized in 1994 as a New Martyr of Russia, he gave his life for the faith in 1938.

The founding congregation of Saints Peter and Paul — approximately 500 Eastern Rite faithful who withdrew to preserve their Byzantine liturgical heritage and formed the parish in 1907.

In 1908, Rev. Alexander Nemolovsky became the first permanent pastor, residing at 131 Grand Street. Having outgrown its house church, the congregation sought to acquire the vacant First Reformed Church of Jersey City on Grand Street, which had consolidated with the Wayne Street Reformed Church. In 1909, the Russian Orthodox Archdiocese in America purchased the 111–113 Grand Street property for $35,000 from the Collegiate Reformed Church of New York City, which had held the mortgage since the 1850s.

Redesigned in the Traditional Russian Orthodox Manner

The Royal Doors and Iconostasis, 1909.

With the parish's formal incorporation on August 31, 1909, work began to transform the Gothic Revival structure into a space fitting for the celebration of the Orthodox liturgy. On July 7, 1909, a hand-carved solid oak Iconostasis — an icon screen — was purchased for $1,200. Its center Royal Doors with icons and red curtains were modeled after those at Saint Nicholas Cathedral in New York City, and its oil-painted icons on canvas were imported from Russia.

The Iconostasis, set on a platform reached by stairs, divides the altar from the nave, symbolically separating the Divine from the human. An icon of the Last Supper appears over the Royal Doors. The Table of Oblation, to the left of the Iconostasis, holds sacred vessels for the Holy Eucharist.

The hand-carved solid oak Iconostasis, purchased in 1909 for $1,200 and modeled after Saint Nicholas Cathedral in New York City. Oil-painted icons on canvas were imported from Russia. The Royal Doors at center are flanked by icons and hung with red curtains.

Russian-style copper domes and Orthodox three-bar crosses cap the towers.

The church's exterior retains the original features of the sandstone Gothic Revival structure. Two elongated pier buttresses accentuate the corner towers and lend the building its heightened symmetrical appearance. The towers are capped with three Russian-style cupolas, copper domes, and Orthodox three-bar crosses symbolizing the Trinity.

The rectangular doorway features a pointed-arch entrance with ornamental molding surround, hood molding, and label stops. The teak doors — with carved Orthodox cross — were imported from Hong Kong and installed in 1965. In 2009, the church's name in gold letters was installed across the building's façade between the molding, above the entrance and below the foiled-arch Gothic stained-glass window.

One of 70+ murals by iconographer Photius Bodasiuk, completed in the 1940s.

On the interior, the original Gothic arches and poured-cement columns remain. A new spruce ceiling modified the former trusses to allow for overhead murals — a hallmark of Orthodox sacred spaces. The more than 70 murals, with decorative stenciled borders on the walls of the nave and ceiling, are the work of iconographer Photius Bodasiuk of Kiev, completed over three years in the 1940s.

The 45,000-crystal chandelier draws the eye toward the altar.

A chandelier of 45,000 crystals imported from abroad runs the length of the center aisle. Smaller chandeliers of matching design illuminate the side aisles. The well-worn dark wood pews remain from the original Dutch Reformed Church.

Over the years, the parish acquired additional lots on Grand Street. In 1960, the present rectory — designed by architect Maurice Kraut — replaced the 1916 rectory at 107 Grand Street and the former Lyceum lot at 109 Grand Street. The parish received funding from the New Jersey Historic Trust for a three-phase, $1.1 million restoration plan from 2000 to 2006, restoring the church to its 1924 appearance in time for the centennial celebration in 2007.
