Our Story

From immigrant chapel to Cathedral

A community shaped by faith, migration, and the permanence of liturgical life

Saints Peter and Paul Orthodox Cathedral began as so many parishes do — not with a building, but with a gathering. In 1907, immigrants arriving in Jersey City from the Austro-Hungarian and Russian Empires found one another in the way immigrants always have: through language, through memory, and through the shared instinct to pray together in the way they had always prayed.

These were people who carried Orthodoxy not as a belief system but as a way of life — a calendar that structured every season, a liturgical rhythm that marked every week, saints whose names they bore and whose stories they knew. When they built their parish, they were not creating something new. They were transplanting something ancient into new soil.

Parish History

1907
Parish Established
Immigrants from the Austro-Hungarian and Russian Empires form a parish community in Jersey City, New Jersey. The parish gathers under the care of the Orthodox Church in America.
1909
A Permanent Home
The parish purchases the former First Reformed Church of Jersey City at 111–113 Grand Street for $35,000. The building — with its distinctive architecture and copper-domed towers — becomes the parish's permanent home.
1920
The Bells
Three bells are purchased from Meneely & Company for $1,705. Their voices have called the faithful to worship for over a century, and continue to ring today over the streets of Jersey City.
1960
Graceland Memorial Park
The parish acquires Graceland Memorial Park, providing a place of Christian burial for parishioners and their families — a ministry the Church has offered since its earliest centuries.
2020
Elevation to Cathedral
Saints Peter and Paul is elevated to the rank of Cathedral — the seat of the Diocese of New York and New Jersey of the Orthodox Church in America. A century of parish life becomes the foundation of something still larger.
Today
115 Years and Still Gathering
Under the pastoral leadership of Rev. Solomon Longo, the Cathedral continues its ministry — welcoming inquirers, forming parishioners, and offering the ancient liturgy every Saturday evening and Sunday morning to a Jersey City that keeps changing around it.

The Building

The church at 111–113 Grand Street is one of Jersey City's most distinctive ecclesiastical buildings. Its copper domes — now a deep verdigris from more than a century of weathering — are visible from several blocks away, a quiet landmark in a neighborhood that has seen waves of change.

The building was originally the First Reformed Church of Jersey City. When the Orthodox community purchased it in 1909, they inherited its solid bones and transformed its interior into the world they had brought with them: iconostasis, candlelight, incense, and the great golden dome of Orthodox sacred space.

To walk through those doors is to experience what the Orthodox tradition has always intended: architecture that orients the body toward heaven, space that makes silence feel inhabited, and light that filters through in a way that makes the ordinary feel sacramental.
