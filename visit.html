A note from our Rector

I am glad you found us.

If you have never been to an Orthodox service before, you might feel a little lost your first time -- and that is completely fine. Most of the people sitting around you felt the same way once. Nobody will quiz you or ask why you are here.

Divine Liturgy on Sunday morning is where we start. Come, sit, observe. If the chanting moves you, let it. If you have questions afterward, I would genuinely like to talk.

Fr. Solomon Longo, Rector
Your First Visit What to know before you arrive
01

When to come

Vespers Saturday at 5:00 PM
Divine Liturgy Sunday at 9:30 AM
Coffee Hour Follows every Sunday Liturgy
Office Monday through Friday, 9 AM to 5 PM

If it is your first time, Sunday Liturgy is the natural place to start. Vespers on Saturday evening is shorter and quieter -- about 45 to 60 minutes -- and a good introduction if you want to ease in.

02

What happens during the service

Divine Liturgy runs about an hour and twenty minutes. Most of it is sung or chanted by the choir in English, with some Greek and Slavonic. You will smell incense and see the clergy process with the Gospel and the holy gifts.

You do not need to follow along perfectly. Bulletins are available at the entrance with the day's readings and hymns.

Holy Communion is received by Orthodox Christians who have prepared through fasting and confession. If you are not Orthodox, you are welcome to come forward for a blessing at the end of the service -- simply cross your arms over your chest.

03

What to wear and bring

Modest, respectful attire. There is no strict dress code, and you will be welcomed regardless of what you wear. That said, this is a formal act of worship, and most parishioners dress accordingly.

Women Traditionally cover their heads inside the church. Visitors are not expected to do so.
Men Typically remove hats before entering.
Children Families with children are welcome. We have a small area near the back if you need space.

You do not need to bring anything. Just show up.

Orthodox Catechesis The journey of becoming Orthodox · 3,000 years in five moments
The Journey of Formation

What does it mean
to become Orthodox?

Catechesis is as old as the Shema. These five moments trace the thread from ancient Israel to the parish on Grand Street.

I c. 1400 BCE · Old Testament

The Ancient Command: Teach Your Children Diligently

The Shema — Hear, O Israel — is the oldest catechetical formula in the Judeo-Christian world. Moses commanded parents to teach the faith at home, on the road, lying down and rising up. The entire book of Deuteronomy is one sustained act of catechesis: salvation history retold so the next generation might receive it as their own.

II c. 50–215 AD · New Testament & Early Church

The Pattern of the First Christians: Road, Table, Mission

The Didache — the oldest surviving Christian catechetical text — presents a moral instruction followed by directives on baptism, Eucharist, and prayer. The Emmaus road narrative gave the Church its catechetical pattern: scripture opened, community gathered, sacrament received, mission sent. To become a Christian was not a transaction. It was a transformation.

III c. 350 AD · Jerusalem

St. Cyril and the Art of Mystagogy

St. Cyril of Jerusalem did not explain the sacraments to candidates before they experienced them. He walked them into the water, anointed them, placed the Body and Blood in their hands — and only then unfolded the meaning of what they had lived. This is mystagogy: leading the newly illumined deeper into the mystery they have already entered. It is a pattern still practiced in this parish today.

IV 19th–20th Century · Russia & America

A Tradition That Traveled: From Moscow to Jersey City

St. Philaret of Moscow's Catechism followed Orthodox immigrants across the Atlantic. Those immigrants built parishes like this one. When Fr. Alexander Schmemann and Fr. Thomas Hopko articulated an Orthodox theology of formation for the OCA in the 1970s, they were not inventing something new — they were recovering something ancient and making it legible in English, for Americans choosing Orthodoxy with open eyes.

V Today · Saints Peter & Paul Cathedral

What Catechesis Looks Like Here

While the OCA prescribes no mandatory format for catechesis, Fr. Solomon offers formal catechesis classes throughout the liturgical year for those preparing to enter the Church. A catechumen attends Liturgy regularly, participates in classes alongside others on the same journey, meets with Fr. Solomon for personal instruction, and receives a prayer and fasting rule suited to their life. The sacramental life of the parish opens at the pace their heart requires.

The ancient pattern — scripture, community, sacrament, mission — is the same one the Church has kept since the road to Emmaus. We are glad you are here, and glad to walk it with you.
